How compound interest actually works — with real numbers

Part of the FirePath Learning Lab · Educational only, not financial advice · Browse all topics →

The Most Powerful Force in Personal Finance

Albert Einstein allegedly called compound interest the "eighth wonder of the world." Whether he actually said it or not, the sentiment holds up. Compound interest is the single most important concept for any Australian building long-term wealth — yet most of us were never properly taught how it works.

The good news is that once you understand it with real numbers, it becomes impossible to ignore. And the earlier you grasp it, the more it works in your favour.

Simple Interest vs Compound Interest: The Key Difference

Before we get to the numbers, it helps to understand the distinction between simple interest and compound interest.

With simple interest, you earn a return only on your original amount. If you invest $10,000 at 7% simple interest, you earn $700 every year — no more, no less. After 10 years, you have $17,000.

With compound interest, you earn a return on your original amount plus all the interest you've already earned. Your interest earns interest. That difference, over time, is enormous.

The core idea: Compound interest means your money grows on top of its own previous growth. Each year's gains become the starting point for the next year's gains.

The Real Numbers: $10,000 at 7% Over Time

Let's use a straightforward example. You invest $10,000 as a lump sum and achieve a 7% average annual return — a historically reasonable long-run figure for a diversified Australian share portfolio, roughly in line with the long-term average of the ASX 200 including dividends (though past performance never guarantees future results).

Here's what compound growth looks like over different timeframes:

  • After 10 years: $19,672 — your money has nearly doubled
  • After 20 years: $38,697 — almost four times your original investment
  • After 30 years: $76,123 — more than seven and a half times your starting amount

Notice the pattern. The first 10 years added roughly $9,700. The second 10 years added about $19,000. The third 10 years added nearly $37,500. The same 10-year period produces more than three times the dollar growth at the end than it did at the beginning. That's compounding at work — the longer it runs, the faster it accelerates.

Compare this to simple interest at the same rate: after 30 years you'd have just $31,000. Compounding delivers more than twice that result from the same starting point and same rate.

Why Starting Early Matters More Than the Amount You Invest

This is where compounding gets genuinely counterintuitive. Consider two Australians: Priya and Jordan.

  • Priya starts investing at age 25. She puts in $10,000 and never adds another dollar. By age 65 (40 years at 7%), she has approximately $149,745.
  • Jordan waits until age 35 to start. He also invests $10,000 and never adds another dollar. By age 65 (30 years at 7%), he has approximately $76,123.

Priya ends up with nearly twice as much money, despite investing the exact same amount. The only difference is that she started 10 years earlier. That decade of early growth compounds relentlessly for the remaining 30 years.

Time in the market is more powerful than the size of your initial investment. A smaller amount invested early will often outperform a larger amount invested late. This is why financial advisers consistently urge Australians not to delay — every year of delay is a year of compounding lost forever.

This principle applies directly to superannuation. Your compulsory super contributions — currently 11.5% of your salary under Australia's Superannuation Guarantee — are quietly compounding inside your fund for decades. The decisions you make in your 20s and 30s about which fund to choose, and whether to make voluntary contributions, can easily be worth hundreds of thousands of dollars by retirement. ASIC's MoneySmart calculator is a free tool that lets you model this for your own situation.

The Rule of 72: A Simple Mental Shortcut

You don't need a spreadsheet to estimate how long it takes your money to double. The Rule of 72 is a quick mental calculation used by investors and financial professionals around the world.

Simply divide 72 by your annual interest rate, and the result is approximately how many years it takes to double your money.

  • At 7% return: 72 ÷ 7 = about 10.3 years to double
  • At 4% return: 72 ÷ 4 = 18 years to double
  • At 2% return: 72 ÷ 2 = 36 years to double
  • At 10% return: 72 ÷ 10 = 7.2 years to double

The Rule of 72 also works in reverse — it shows you how quickly inflation erodes your purchasing power. If inflation runs at 3% (close to the RBA's target band of 2–3%), your money's purchasing power halves in roughly 24 years. This is one of the core reasons that simply holding cash long-term is a financial risk of its own.

Saving in a Bank vs Investing: The Real-World Gap

Most Australians keep the bulk of their savings in a bank account. It feels safe, and it is — deposits up to $250,000 per institution are protected under the Australian Government's Financial Claims Scheme. But safety comes at a cost.

As of 2024, even the most competitive high-interest savings accounts in Australia offer rates in the range of 4.5–5.5%. That sounds reasonable, but consider two important factors:

Interest is taxed as income

The ATO treats interest earned in a savings account as ordinary income. If you're on a marginal tax rate of 32.5% (which applies to income between $45,001 and $120,000), a 5% savings rate becomes roughly 3.4% after tax. At 45%, it drops closer to 2.75%.

Inflation reduces real returns

Once you subtract inflation from that already-taxed return, your real (after-inflation, after-tax) return on a savings account can be very close to zero — or even negative in periods of higher inflation.

A long-term diversified investment in Australian and international shares, by contrast, has historically delivered average real returns well above cash. Returns from shares are also often more tax-efficient for Australian investors, thanks to franking credits on Australian dividends — a system where companies pass on tax they've already paid to shareholders, reducing the investor's own tax bill.
