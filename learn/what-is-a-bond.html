What is a bond and why it matters for your portfolio

Part of the FirePath Learning Lab · Educational only, not financial advice · Browse all topics →

The Simple Version: You're the Bank

When most Australians think about investing, they think about shares — buying a small piece of a company and hoping it grows in value. Bonds work differently. When you buy a bond, you're not buying ownership of anything. You're lending money to a government or a company, and they're promising to pay it back — with interest.

Think of it like this: if your neighbour asked to borrow $10,000 and agreed to pay you $400 every year for five years, then return the full $10,000 at the end, that's essentially how a bond works. You're the lender. The borrower pays you regular interest (called a coupon), and at the end of the agreed period (called the maturity date), you get your original money back.

Governments issue bonds to fund things like hospitals, roads, and schools. Companies issue bonds when they want to raise money without selling shares to new investors. Either way, the bond is a formal legal contract — a promise to pay.

How the Interest Actually Works

When a bond is created, it has a face value (typically $1,000 per bond), a fixed interest rate, and an end date. If a bond has a face value of $1,000 and pays 4% per year, you receive $40 annually until the bond matures. Simple enough.

However, bonds are also traded on financial markets, which means their price can go up and down before the maturity date. This is where things get slightly more complex — but it's worth understanding because it directly affects how bond funds behave in your portfolio.

Key insight: When interest rates rise, existing bond prices fall. When interest rates fall, existing bond prices rise. This inverse relationship is one of the most important concepts in fixed income investing.

Here's why: imagine you hold a bond paying 3% interest. Then the Reserve Bank of Australia (RBA) raises rates and new bonds are now being issued at 5%. Your 3% bond suddenly looks much less attractive, so its market price drops to compensate. Conversely, if rates fall to 1%, your 3% bond looks very appealing and its price rises. This is the fundamental tension in bond investing.

Why Bonds Are Generally Lower Risk Than Shares

Bonds aren't risk-free — no investment is — but they tend to be significantly more stable than shares for a few important reasons.

  • You're a creditor, not an owner: If a company goes under, bondholders get paid before shareholders do. Shareholders often receive nothing in a bankruptcy; bondholders often recover at least some of their money.
  • Predictable income: Unlike share dividends, which companies can cut at any time, bond payments are legally contracted. You know exactly what you're supposed to receive and when.
  • Lower volatility: Bond prices don't swing as wildly as share prices. Your portfolio won't drop 35% in a month the way a share-heavy portfolio can during a crisis.
  • Defensive in downturns: During recessions or sharemarket crashes, investors often move money into bonds (especially government bonds), which can actually push bond prices up when shares are falling.

That last point is important for portfolio construction. Bonds and shares often move in opposite directions, which means holding both can smooth out your overall returns over time.

Government Bonds vs Corporate Bonds

Not all bonds carry the same level of risk. It's worth understanding the two main types available to Australian investors.

Government Bonds

Australian Government Bonds (also called Commonwealth Government Securities or CGS) are issued by the Australian federal government. These are considered among the safest investments available — the Australian government has never defaulted on its debt. State governments also issue bonds. The trade-off for this safety is a lower interest rate. You won't get rich from government bonds, but you're very unlikely to lose your principal.

Corporate Bonds

Corporate bonds are issued by companies like the big banks, Telstra, or Woolworths. Because companies carry more risk of default than governments, they must offer higher interest rates to attract lenders. This extra return is called the credit spread.

Corporate bonds are further divided by credit quality. High-quality companies (called investment grade) offer modest rates with relatively low risk. Lower-quality companies (sometimes called high yield or, less flatteringly, junk bonds) offer higher rates but with meaningfully higher default risk. For most everyday Australian investors, sticking to investment-grade bonds is the sensible approach.

Worth knowing: ASIC's MoneySmart website has a straightforward explainer on bonds and fixed income that's worth reading if you want to dig deeper into how credit ratings and bond risk are assessed.

How Australian Investors Can Actually Buy Bonds

Buying individual bonds used to be complicated and expensive — largely the domain of institutional investors and the very wealthy. That's changed significantly. Today, most everyday Australian investors access bonds through Exchange Traded Funds (ETFs) listed on the ASX.

Bond ETFs work just like share ETFs — you buy units on the ASX through your brokerage account, and the fund holds a diversified basket of bonds on your behalf. You receive regular income distributions (which the ATO treats as interest income, not dividends, so there are no franking credits), and you can buy or sell on any trading day.

Some of the broad categories of bond ETFs available on the ASX include:

  • Australian government bond ETFs — holding Commonwealth and state government bonds
  • Australian composite bond ETFs — mixing government and investment-grade corporate bonds
  • Global bond ETFs — providing exposure to international government and corporate bonds, sometimes currency-hedged back to Australian dollars
  • Short-duration bond ETFs — holding bonds that mature sooner, which are less sensitive to interest rate changes

It's worth comparing expense ratios (the annual management fee) across these products, as fees directly reduce your returns. Many Australian bond ETFs have management fees ranging from around 0.10% to 0.30% per year, which is quite reasonable.

Bonds in a FIRE Portfolio: When Do They Make Sense?

The FIRE movement (Financial Independence, Retire Early) tends to be dominated by growth-focused investors — heavy on shares, light on bonds. That approach makes sense during the accumulation phase when you have decades to ride out market volatility. But as you approach and enter early retirement, bonds become much more relevant for a specific reason: sequence of returns risk.

Sequence of returns risk is the danger that a major market crash occurs right at the beginning of your retirement, forcing you to sell shares at low prices to fund living expenses. This can permanently damage a portfolio in a way that a mid-career crash simply doesn't. Having a meaningful allocation to bonds means you can draw down on those more stable assets during a downturn, giving your share portfolio time to recover.

Inside your superannuation fund, you've likely already had some bond exposure — balanced and growth options typically include a blend of shares, property, and fixed income. As you approach your preservation age and retirement, most super funds automatically shift toward more conservative allocations with higher bond weightings. Understanding why this happens helps you make more deliberate choices about your super investment option.

A common framework is to hold enough bonds (or other defensive assets) outside super to cover two to three years of living expenses in early retirement. This acts as a buffer so you're never forced to sell shares in a downturn.
