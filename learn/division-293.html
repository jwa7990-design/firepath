🧾 Tax & Strategy · Superannuation

Division 293 tax, explained

Salary sacrifice usually means paying just 15% tax on contributions instead of your marginal rate. Above a certain income, that concession gets partially clawed back — but it's rarely enough to make salary sacrifice not worth it.

The whole appeal of concessional super contributions is the tax gap between your marginal rate and the 15% rate applied inside super. Division 293 exists to narrow that gap for high earners, on the reasoning that the tax concession is worth proportionally more to people already on the top marginal rates.

The threshold and the extra tax

If your income plus your concessional super contributions for the year exceed $250,000, an additional 15% tax applies to the portion of your contributions above that threshold — effectively lifting the tax rate on those contributions from 15% to 30%.

This $250,000 threshold is set in legislation and hasn't been indexed since it was reduced from $300,000 in 2017 — meaning as wages grow over time, more people gradually fall into Division 293 territory without the threshold moving.

Worked example
Someone earning $230,000 in salary decides to salary sacrifice the full concessional cap of $32,500 in 2026–27. Their combined income plus contributions is approximately $262,500 — $12,500 over the $250,000 threshold. Division 293 applies an extra 15% tax on that $12,500 portion of contributions, an additional tax bill of $1,875.

Is it still worth salary sacrificing?

Almost always, yes. Even with Division 293 pushing the effective rate to 30% on the affected portion, that's still meaningfully lower than the top marginal rate of 45% plus the 2% Medicare levy. For someone on the top bracket, the net benefit of salary sacrificing drops from roughly 32 cents in the dollar (47% − 15%) to about 17 cents in the dollar (47% − 30%) — reduced, but still a real benefit, not eliminated.

What counts toward the $250,000

"Division 293 income" is a specific calculation — broadly your taxable income, plus reportable fringe benefits, plus net investment losses, plus low-tax contributions (your concessional contributions, minus any excess). It's not simply your gross salary, so someone with significant salary sacrifice, investment losses, or fringe benefits might trigger Division 293 at a lower headline salary than expected.

How and when it's charged

You don't pay this at the time contributions are made — the ATO issues a separate Division 293 assessment after you lodge your tax return, once your full income picture for the year is known. You can choose to pay it from your own funds or have it released from your super balance.

The bigger picture

Division 293 is a narrowing of the concession, not a removal of it. For most people near the threshold, it's worth continuing to salary sacrifice up to the cap — the tax saving is reduced but still meaningfully positive, and the money is still compounding inside a lower-tax environment than it would outside super.
