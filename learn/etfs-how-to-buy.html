ETFs — what they are, how to buy them, and what they cost

Part of the FirePath Learning Lab · Educational only, not financial advice · Browse all topics →

What Is an ETF?

An ETF, or Exchange Traded Fund, is a type of investment fund that holds a collection of assets — such as shares, bonds, or commodities — and trades on a stock exchange just like an ordinary share. When you buy units in an ETF, you're buying a small slice of everything that fund holds.

For example, an ETF tracking the Australian share market might hold shares in hundreds of Australian companies simultaneously. Rather than researching and buying each company individually, you make a single purchase and gain exposure to all of them at once.

ETFs vs Managed Funds — What's the Difference?

Both ETFs and managed funds pool money from many investors to buy a basket of assets, but there are some important practical differences:

  • Trading: ETFs trade on the ASX throughout the day, just like shares in Commonwealth Bank or BHP. Managed funds typically price once per day after the market closes, and you transact directly with the fund manager.
  • Minimum investment: Many managed funds require a minimum investment of $1,000–$5,000 or more. ETFs can often be purchased for the price of a single unit, which might be $50–$150 depending on the fund.
  • Transparency: Most ETFs publish their holdings daily, so you can see exactly what you own. Managed funds often disclose holdings less frequently.
  • Costs: ETFs generally have lower ongoing fees than actively managed funds, though you also pay brokerage when buying and selling.

Most ETFs listed on the ASX are passively managed — they aim to track an index (like the ASX 200) rather than beat the market. This keeps costs low, though actively managed ETFs also exist.

How to Buy an ETF in Australia

Step 1 — Open a Brokerage Account

To buy ETFs on the ASX, you need a brokerage account (also called a share trading account). These are offered by banks, online brokers, and financial platforms. ASIC regulates these providers, so look for an Australian Financial Services (AFS) licence holder.

When comparing brokers, consider brokerage fees per trade, the platform's usability, whether they offer Chess Sponsored holdings (which means the shares are registered in your name directly with ASX Settlement), and any ongoing account fees.

Step 2 — Search for the ETF by Its ASX Code

Every ETF listed on the ASX has a unique ticker code — usually three or four letters. Once you know the code of the ETF you want, you can search for it within your broker's platform to see the current price and place an order.

Step 3 — Place Your Order

You'll typically choose between two order types:

  • Market order: You buy immediately at whatever the current market price is. Fast and simple, but you have less control over the exact price you pay.
  • Limit order: You set the maximum price you're willing to pay. Your order will only execute if the ETF reaches that price. Useful in volatile markets.

Understanding the Bid/Ask Spread

When you look up an ETF on the ASX, you'll see two prices: the bid (the highest price a buyer is currently offering) and the ask (the lowest price a seller is currently accepting). The difference between these two is called the spread.

A tighter spread means lower hidden costs for you. Larger, more popular ETFs tend to have tighter spreads because there are more buyers and sellers active at any time. Less liquid ETFs can have wider spreads, meaning you effectively pay a small premium to buy and receive slightly less when you sell.

The bid/ask spread is a real cost of trading ETFs that's easy to overlook. For long-term investors making infrequent purchases, it's usually minor — but worth understanding before you trade.

What Does an ETF Actually Cost?

Management Expense Ratio (MER)

The management expense ratio, or MER, is the annual fee charged by the fund manager to operate the ETF. It's expressed as a percentage of your investment and is deducted from the fund's assets automatically — you don't receive a separate bill. If an ETF has an MER of 0.20%, you pay $20 per year for every $10,000 invested.

Broad market index ETFs in Australia tend to have MERs ranging from roughly 0.05% to 0.25% per year. International or more specialised ETFs often charge more. Actively managed ETFs typically charge higher fees than passive index-tracking ones.

Brokerage Costs

Each time you buy or sell an ETF, your broker charges a brokerage fee. These can range from a few dollars per trade on low-cost platforms to $20 or more on some traditional bank platforms. For investors making small, regular contributions, brokerage costs can eat into returns significantly — so factor this in when deciding how often to invest.

Other Costs to Be Aware Of

  • Currency hedging costs: Some international ETFs hedge their currency exposure back to Australian dollars. This reduces the impact of AUD/USD movements but adds a small additional cost.
  • Tax drag: ETFs that hold international assets may face withholding taxes on dividends paid in other countries before those earnings reach you.

Dividends and Dividend Reinvestment Plans (DRP)

Most ETFs distribute income to investors periodically — often quarterly or semi-annually. This income might come from dividends paid by the underlying shares, interest from bonds, or other earnings. These are called distributions.

Many ETFs offer a Dividend Reinvestment Plan (DRP), which automatically uses your distributions to buy additional units in the ETF rather than paying cash into your account. This is a convenient way to compound your investment over time without paying additional brokerage, though not all ETFs or brokers offer this feature.

Even if you reinvest distributions through a DRP, the ATO still treats them as taxable income in the year they're received. Keep records of all distributions for your tax return.

Tax Considerations for Australian Investors

Distributions and Income Tax

Distributions from Australian share ETFs often include franking credits — tax credits attached to dividends paid by Australian companies that have already paid corporate tax. These credits can reduce your personal income tax liability or even generate a refund, depending on your tax rate. You'll receive an annual tax statement from the ETF provider detailing all distributions and any franking credits.

Capital Gains Tax (CGT)

When you sell ETF units for more than you paid, you make a capital gain, which is generally subject to Capital Gains Tax. The ATO treats ETF units as assets under the standard CGT rules. Importantly, if you hold your units for more than 12 months before selling, you may be eligible for the 50% CGT discount, meaning only half your gain is added to your taxable income.

ETFs can also distribute capital gains to investors at year end — particularly if the fund has sold assets internally. These appear in your annual tax statement and are assessable even if you didn't sell any of your own units.
