Property investing explained — leverage, equity, and cash flow

Part of the FirePath Learning Lab · Educational only, not financial advice · Browse all topics →

How Property Investing Works

Property investing means buying real estate — typically residential houses, units, or townhouses — with the intention of generating a financial return. That return can come from two sources: rental income (money paid by tenants each week) and capital growth (the property increasing in value over time).

The basic process looks like this: you purchase a property, find a tenant, collect rent, and eventually sell the property at a higher price than you paid. Simple in theory — but the details matter enormously, and the costs involved are significant.

Rental Income

Tenants pay rent, which becomes your income. From that income, you'll pay expenses including your mortgage interest, council rates, insurance, property management fees, repairs, and maintenance. What's left — if anything — is your net rental income. The Australian Taxation Office (ATO) treats rental income as taxable, but also allows you to deduct legitimate expenses against it.

Capital Growth

Over time, property values can rise. If you buy a property for $700,000 and sell it for $950,000 ten years later, that $250,000 gain is called a capital gain. In Australia, if you've held the property for more than 12 months, you're entitled to a 50% capital gains tax (CGT) discount — meaning only half the gain is added to your taxable income for that year. This is a meaningful tax advantage worth understanding.

Leverage — Borrowing to Invest

One of the most distinctive features of property investing is leverage — using borrowed money (a mortgage) to control a much larger asset than you could afford outright. This is what makes property different from simply putting cash in a savings account.

Here's a straightforward example: you have $150,000 in savings and use it as a deposit to purchase a $600,000 property. You've borrowed $450,000 from the bank. If the property grows in value by 10% (to $660,000), your $60,000 gain represents a 40% return on your original $150,000 — not 10%. That amplification is leverage at work.

The leverage double-edge: Leverage amplifies gains, but it equally amplifies losses. If that same property fell 10% in value, you'd lose $60,000 — 40% of your initial investment — while still owing the full mortgage. The bank doesn't share your loss.

Australian lenders typically require a deposit of at least 10–20% of the purchase price. If your deposit is below 20%, most lenders will charge Lenders Mortgage Insurance (LMI) — a one-off premium that can run into the thousands and protects the lender (not you) if you default.

Understanding Equity

Equity is the portion of your property that you actually own — the difference between what the property is worth and what you still owe the bank. If your property is worth $700,000 and your outstanding mortgage is $450,000, your equity is $250,000.

As you pay down the loan and/or the property value rises, your equity grows. Many investors then use this equity as security to borrow again and purchase additional properties — a strategy sometimes called "recycling equity." This can accelerate portfolio building, but it also means you're taking on more debt and more risk across multiple properties.

Rental Yield — What the Numbers Actually Look Like

Rental yield is a key measure of how much income a property generates relative to its price. There are two versions:

  • Gross rental yield: Annual rent ÷ Property value × 100. A property worth $600,000 renting for $500 per week earns $26,000 per year — a gross yield of about 4.3%.
  • Net rental yield: Subtracts all costs (management fees, rates, insurance, maintenance) before dividing. Net yields are typically 1–2% lower than gross yields and give a more honest picture.

In most major Australian cities, gross rental yields currently sit between 3–5%. In some regional areas they can be higher, but regional markets carry different risks including thinner tenant demand and less liquidity.

Negative Gearing vs Positive Gearing

These terms describe whether your investment property costs you money each year or puts money in your pocket.

Negative Gearing

A property is negatively geared when your rental income is less than your total expenses (including mortgage interest). You're making a loss on the property — but under Australian tax law, you can offset that loss against your other income, reducing your income tax bill. This is why negatively geared properties are most attractive to high-income earners in upper tax brackets. The ATO essentially subsidises part of your holding costs, but you're still losing money — you're simply losing less of it after tax. The strategy only pays off if capital growth eventually compensates for those accumulated losses.

Positive Gearing

A property is positively geared when rental income exceeds all expenses. You're making a profit on the property — which is taxable, but leaves you with actual cash in hand. Positive cash flow properties can be harder to find in capital cities at current price levels, but they do exist, particularly in higher-yield regional or interstate markets.

Neither gearing strategy is universally better. Negative gearing is a bet on capital growth. Positive gearing prioritises cash flow. Your income, tax rate, financial goals, and risk tolerance should drive the decision — not convention.

The True Cost of Buying Property

Property comes with significant upfront costs that investors sometimes underestimate. Before you've even collected a dollar of rent, you'll typically face:

  • Stamp duty: A state government tax on the purchase price. In New South Wales, buying a $750,000 investment property attracts roughly $29,000 in stamp duty. Rates vary by state and are calculated on a sliding scale.
  • Legal and conveyancing fees: Typically $1,500–$3,000.
  • Building and pest inspections: $400–$800.
  • Loan establishment fees and LMI (if applicable).
  • Immediate repairs or improvements to make the property rentable.

In total, purchasing costs can easily add 4–6% to your effective purchase price. These costs are not deductible upfront — though some (like stamp duty) form part of your cost base for CGT purposes when you eventually sell.

Property Management and Tenancy

Most investors engage a property manager — typically a real estate agency — to handle tenant selection, rent collection, maintenance coordination, and compliance with tenancy law. Fees typically run 7–12% of weekly rent, plus charges for lease renewals, routine inspections, and arranging repairs.

Tenancy law in Australia is state-based, which means your rights and obligations as a landlord differ depending on whether your property is in Queensland, Victoria, Western Australia, or elsewhere. Each state has a residential tenancies authority that governs lease terms, bond handling, rent increases, repairs, and eviction procedures. Understanding the rules in your state is not optional — breaching them can be costly.

Vacancy periods — weeks or months without a tenant — are a real risk. Even a two-week vacancy on a $500/week property costs you $1,000 in lost income, while most of your expenses continue regardless.

Property vs Shares — An Honest Comparison

Australian investors often debate property versus the ASX (Australian Securities Exchange). Both are legitimate wealth-building vehicles.
