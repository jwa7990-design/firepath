First Home Super Saver Scheme

If you're saving for your first home, super's lower tax rate can be used deliberately — contribute extra, let it grow inside super, then withdraw it later specifically for a deposit.

Normally, money in super is locked away until you reach preservation age. The First Home Super Saver Scheme (FHSSS) is a specific carve-out — voluntary contributions you make can later be released for a first home deposit, well before retirement.

How much you can put in — and get out

You can contribute up to $15,000 per financial year toward FHSSS, up to a $50,000 lifetime maximum across all years since 1 July 2017. Each person in a couple has their own separate $50,000 entitlement — meaning a couple buying together could access up to $100,000 combined.

These are voluntary contributions only — your employer's compulsory Super Guarantee payments don't count and can't be accessed this way.

What actually comes back out

The releasable amount depends on how the contribution was made:

  1. Non-concessional (after-tax) contributions — 100% releasable
  2. Concessional (salary sacrifice or tax-deductible) contributions — 85% releasable, since 15% was already paid as contributions tax
  3. Plus deemed earnings — a notional growth rate the ATO applies, based on the 90-day bank bill rate plus 3%, not your fund's actual investment return
Worked example
You salary sacrifice $5,000 and separately contribute $3,000 from after-tax savings in the same year. Under FHSSS, you'd be eligible to release 85% of the $5,000 ($4,250) plus 100% of the $3,000 — a combined $7,250, plus whatever deemed earnings accrued while it sat in super.

The tax treatment on the way out

Concessional contributions and their earnings are included in your assessable income when released, but come with a 30% FHSS tax offset — so you effectively pay your marginal rate minus 30% on that portion. For someone on a 32% marginal rate, that works out to roughly 2% tax on withdrawal. Non-concessional contributions come back out completely tax-free, since you'd already paid tax on that money before it went in.

The eligibility rules

You need to be at least 18 when you request the determination, and you must never have owned property in Australia — including investment property, vacant land, or a share of a family home. This is assessed per person, not per couple, so if one partner has owned property before, the other can still use the scheme individually.

⚠️ Timing matters a lot here. Once you request a release, you generally have 12 months to sign a contract to purchase or build. If that timeline is tight, it's worth requesting the determination (which doesn't lock you in) well before you're ready to release the actual funds.

The process, step by step

  1. Make voluntary contributions to super over time (salary sacrifice or personal after-tax)
  2. Request an FHSSS determination from the ATO via myGov — this tells you your maximum releasable amount and doesn't commit you to anything
  3. When ready, submit a release request
  4. Funds typically arrive in your bank account within 15–25 business days (longer during January–March peak periods)
  5. Sign a contract within 12 months, and notify the ATO within 28 days of signing
