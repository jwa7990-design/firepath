Australian shares vs global shares — where should your money live?

Part of the FirePath Learning Lab · Educational only, not financial advice · Browse all topics →

The Home Bias Problem Most Australian Investors Have

Ask a typical Australian investor to show you their portfolio and there's a good chance it's heavily weighted toward Australian shares. This isn't just anecdotal — research consistently shows Australian investors hold far more domestic shares than their share of the global market would justify. This tendency is called home bias, and while it's understandable, it can quietly work against your long-term wealth building.

Here's the number that should stop you in your tracks: the Australian Securities Exchange (ASX) represents roughly 2% of total global share market capitalisation. That means if you held a portfolio that purely mirrored the world's investment opportunities, only about two cents in every dollar would be in Australian companies. Yet many Australian investors — particularly those building toward Financial Independence, Retire Early (FIRE) — hold 50%, 70%, or even 100% of their portfolio in ASX stocks.

This isn't necessarily wrong. There are genuine, legitimate reasons to overweight Australian shares. But every investor should make this decision deliberately, not by default.

Why Australian Shares Have Real Advantages

Franking Credits: Australia's Unique Tax Gift

Australia operates an imputation tax system, which means companies pay tax on their profits before distributing dividends, and then attach "franking credits" to those dividends to reflect the tax already paid. When you receive a fully franked dividend, you can use those credits to offset your personal income tax bill — and if your tax rate is low enough, the ATO may even refund excess credits to you.

For FIRE investors, this is genuinely powerful. Someone who has retired early and has a low taxable income can receive fully franked dividends and potentially receive cash refunds from the ATO for the full value of the attached franking credits. This effectively boosts the real yield on Australian dividend-paying shares well beyond what the headline dividend percentage suggests.

Example: A share paying a 4% fully franked dividend is equivalent to roughly a 5.7% pre-tax yield when you account for the 30% corporate tax already paid. For a retiree in a low tax bracket, much of this tax can be refunded directly.

This advantage doesn't exist with overseas shares. International dividends arrive without franking credits and may even have foreign withholding taxes deducted before you receive them, which adds complexity and can reduce your net return.

No Currency Complexity

When you invest in Australian shares, you invest in Australian dollars, receive dividends in Australian dollars, and spend your retirement income in Australian dollars. There's no conversion, no exchange rate risk, and no added complexity at tax time. For people living in Australia and spending in Australian dollars, this simplicity has real value.

Why Global Shares Deserve a Place in Your Portfolio

Diversification Across Industries and Economies

The ASX is heavily concentrated in just a few sectors. Financials (predominantly the big four banks) and materials (mining companies) together make up well over half the index by market weight. This means an "all-Australian" portfolio is heavily exposed to the health of Australian housing, iron ore prices, and commodity cycles — and relatively light on technology, healthcare innovation, consumer goods, and many other sectors that drive growth globally.

Global share markets, particularly the US, give you access to companies that simply don't exist on the ASX at scale — major technology platforms, pharmaceutical innovators, global consumer brands, and industrial giants. True diversification means spreading your investments not just across companies, but across industries, economies, and currencies.

Historical Returns: The Numbers Don't Favour Home Bias

Over long periods, global share markets — particularly the US market — have delivered strong returns that have often outpaced the ASX on a total return basis. The ASX has historically offered higher dividend yields (boosted by franking credits), while global markets have tended to offer stronger capital growth. Neither is definitively "better" — they complement each other in different ways, and past performance never guarantees future results.

What the data does suggest is that diversifying globally has, over most extended periods, either matched or improved risk-adjusted returns compared to holding only Australian shares. The RBA and academic research both highlight that Australian-only portfolios carry concentrated country risk that investors often underestimate.

Understanding Currency Risk With Global Shares

When you invest in international shares, you're exposed to currency risk — the possibility that exchange rate movements will affect your returns when converted back to Australian dollars. If the Australian dollar strengthens against the US dollar, your US share returns are worth less when you bring them home. If the AUD weakens, your returns look better.

This gives rise to two approaches:

  • Unhedged international funds leave you exposed to currency fluctuations. Over the long term, many investors accept this as a natural form of additional diversification — your portfolio partially benefits when Australia's economy struggles and the AUD weakens.
  • Hedged international funds use financial instruments to neutralise currency movements, so your return closely mirrors the underlying share market performance in AUD terms. This removes currency volatility but comes with a small ongoing cost and removes the potential benefit of AUD weakness.

For long-term FIRE investors, many financial educators suggest unhedged global exposure may actually complement Australian assets well — when local conditions deteriorate and the AUD falls, your global holdings naturally increase in value, providing a buffer. However, this is a personal decision based on your timeline, risk tolerance, and income needs.

It's worth noting that currency risk matters more in the short term. Over decades, the compounding growth of global businesses tends to dwarf currency movements in significance.

What a Balanced Approach Looks Like

Most thoughtful FIRE investors in Australia don't choose between Australian and global shares — they hold both, in proportions they've deliberately considered. The debate isn't really "which one" but "how much of each."

Common frameworks you'll encounter in the Australian FIRE community include:

  • The pure global market weight approach: Hold approximately 98% global, 2% Australian — matching actual market capitalisation. Very few Australian investors adopt this extreme, given the real franking credit advantage they'd be forgoing.
  • The 70/30 global split: Roughly 70% global shares, 30% Australian shares. This acknowledges the size of global markets while still capturing meaningful franking credit benefits.
  • The 50/50 split: Equal weighting between Australian and international. Popular among income-focused FIRE investors who particularly value the reliability of franked dividends in drawdown.
  • The Australian-heavy approach: 70–80% ASX, balance in international. Common among investors prioritising simplicity and income yield, particularly those closer to or in early retirement where cash flow matters most.

There is no universally correct answer. Your ideal split depends on your tax situation, how much you value dividend income versus capital growth, your timeline to FIRE, and your comfort with currency movements.

Super: Don't Forget Your Largest Investment

For most Australians, superannuation is their largest asset. Many default super funds (and industry funds regulated by APRA) already hold significant international exposure in their balanced or growth options. Before obsessing over the split in your personal investment portfolio, check what your super is already doing — you may be more globally diversified than you realise, or you may find your total picture is more concentrated than intended.

ASIC's MoneySmart resources are a helpful starting point for understanding how to read your super statement and assess your overall asset allocation across both super and non-super investments.
