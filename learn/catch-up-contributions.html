Catch-up concessional contributions — how they actually work

If your super balance is on the lower side and you've had a few years of not maxing out your cap, there's a mechanism that lets you put in a lot more than $32,500 in a single year — without paying extra tax.

Every financial year, there's a limit on how much can go into your super at the concessional (before-tax) rate — this includes your employer's compulsory contributions, any salary sacrifice, and any personal contributions you claim as a tax deduction. For 2026–27, that general concessional contributions cap is $32,500.

But if you haven't used your full cap in previous years, the ATO doesn't just let that room disappear. It's tracked, and under certain conditions, you can carry it forward and use it later — potentially all in one go.

Who actually qualifies

Two conditions need to be true at the same time:

Your total super balance was under $500,000 as of 30 June the previous financial year, and you have unused concessional cap from any of the past 5 financial years (only years from 2018–19 onward count).

The $500,000 threshold is checked at a single point in time — the 30 June just gone — not continuously throughout the year. Unused amounts expire after 5 years on a rolling basis, and the oldest year's unused cap gets used first when you go over the standard cap.

What it looks like in practice

Worked example
Say your super balance has only had employer contributions going in — no salary sacrifice — for the past three years, and each year you had roughly $12,000 of unused cap. That's about $36,000 in unused cap sitting there. Combined with this year's $32,500 cap, you could potentially make a concessional contribution of up to $68,500 in a single year without breaching the cap — useful if you've just sold an asset, received a bonus, or had a particularly high-earning year and want to reduce your taxable income while boosting super at the same time.

The tax mechanics stay the same as any concessional contribution — the money is taxed at 15% inside super instead of your marginal rate, which for most middle-to-high income earners is a meaningful gap. On $30 salary sacrificed at a 32% marginal rate versus 15% inside super, that's roughly 17 cents in the dollar difference.

How to check what you've actually got available

  1. Log in to myGov and link the ATO service (or go directly if already linked)
  2. Go to Super → Information
  3. Select Carry-forward concessional contributions
  4. The page will show your unused amounts by year, and confirm whether you're eligible based on your total super balance

One thing worth knowing: if you're in an SMSF or defined benefit fund, the figures shown might lag until your fund's annual return is processed — so the number you see in, say, August might not reflect contributions made just before 30 June.

Timing matters

Because unused cap expires after 5 years, the oldest year's room is always used first. If you're planning to use a windfall (an inheritance, a bonus, proceeds from selling something) to top up super, it's worth checking sooner rather than later whether any of your older unused cap is close to its expiry — using it now versus waiting a year could be the difference between a strategy working and that room disappearing for good.
