Dollar cost averaging — what it is and why it works

Part of the FirePath Learning Lab · Educational only, not financial advice · Browse all topics →

What is dollar cost averaging?

Dollar cost averaging (DCA) is a straightforward investment strategy where you invest a fixed dollar amount at regular intervals — regardless of what the market is doing at the time. Instead of trying to find the perfect moment to invest a large lump sum, you commit to investing, say, $200 every fortnight no matter whether markets are rising, falling, or sitting still.

The name comes from the effect this approach has on your average purchase price over time. Because you're investing the same dollar amount each period, you automatically buy more units when prices are low and fewer units when prices are high. Over time, this tends to bring your average cost per unit down below the simple average of all the prices you paid — which is where the "averaging" part earns its keep.

It's one of the most widely recommended strategies for everyday investors, and for good reason. It's simple, disciplined, and removes a lot of the stress that comes with investing.

How it works in practice

Let's look at a concrete example using a hypothetical ASX exchange-traded fund (ETF) — a fund that tracks a basket of shares and trades on the Australian Securities Exchange like a regular share.

Imagine you invest $500 every month for five months. The unit price of your ETF moves around as markets do:

  • Month 1: Price $25.00 → you buy 20 units
  • Month 2: Price $20.00 → you buy 25 units
  • Month 3: Price $16.67 → you buy 30 units
  • Month 4: Price $20.00 → you buy 25 units
  • Month 5: Price $25.00 → you buy 20 units

Total invested: $2,500. Total units acquired: 120 units. Your average cost per unit: $20.83.

Now notice something important. The simple average of all the prices across those five months is $21.33. But because you bought more units during the dip in months two and three, your actual average cost came out lower — at $20.83. With the price back at $25.00 by month five, your 120 units are worth $3,000, giving you a $500 gain on your $2,500 investment.

The key insight: With dollar cost averaging, a market dip isn't just bad news — it's an opportunity to buy more of what you want at a lower price. The same $500 goes further when prices fall.

Removing emotion from investing

One of the biggest threats to investment returns isn't market volatility — it's investor behaviour. Research consistently shows that ordinary investors tend to buy when markets are rising (feeling confident) and sell when markets are falling (feeling scared). This is the opposite of what rational investing suggests you should do.

The Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC) highlights behavioural biases as a significant risk for retail investors. Fear and greed are powerful forces, and even experienced investors struggle to override them.

Dollar cost averaging sidesteps this problem by removing the decision entirely. You invest the same amount, on the same schedule, every time. There's no agonising over whether now is a good time, no waiting for the market to "calm down," and no temptation to pile in after a strong run. The strategy enforces discipline automatically.

Dollar cost averaging versus lump sum investing

A natural question arises: if you have a larger amount available — say, from an inheritance, a property sale, or a redundancy payment — is it better to invest it all at once or spread it out over time?

The honest answer is that lump sum investing has historically outperformed DCA in markets that trend upward over time, simply because your money is invested and compounding for longer. Research across global markets, including the ASX, supports this view. If you invest $24,000 today rather than $1,000 a month for 24 months, and the market rises steadily, you'll likely end up with more money.

However, lump sum investing comes with a real psychological risk. If you invest everything and the market immediately drops 20%, the emotional pressure to sell can be overwhelming — and selling at a loss locks in that loss permanently. For many people, the theoretical advantage of lump sum investing is undermined by their actual behaviour when things get rough.

DCA offers a middle ground: you're still getting money into the market, you're building the habit of investing, and you're reducing the risk of terrible timing. For most everyday Australian investors who are investing from income rather than a windfall, the question is largely academic anyway — DCA is simply how their financial life works.

Why DCA suits salary earners so naturally

For Australians investing from their regular income, dollar cost averaging isn't just a strategy — it's the natural shape of their financial life. You earn money fortnightly or monthly, you pay your expenses, and you invest what's left (or better yet, what you've set aside first).

This aligns perfectly with how superannuation works. Your employer makes compulsory super contributions — currently 11.5% of your ordinary time earnings under the Superannuation Guarantee — into your fund every pay cycle. That money goes into the market regularly regardless of conditions. In this sense, every working Australian with superannuation is already dollar cost averaging, whether they realise it or not.

The same principle can extend to your investments outside super. Setting up a regular transfer to a brokerage account or managed fund — timed to coincide with your pay cycle — puts DCA to work on your broader wealth-building goals.

Practical tip: Many Australian brokers and investment platforms allow you to automate regular investments into ETFs or managed funds. Automating removes the need for willpower — the investment happens before you have a chance to second-guess it.

The psychological benefits

Beyond the mechanics, there's a quieter benefit to DCA that doesn't show up in spreadsheets: it makes investing less stressful.

When you're invested in the market with a lump sum, every news headline about RBA interest rate decisions, global trade tensions, or ASX volatility can feel personal. Your net worth is fluctuating with every tick, and it's easy to become anxious or reactive.

With a DCA approach, market falls carry a different emotional weight. Yes, your existing units are worth less — but your next scheduled purchase is going to buy more units than it would have last month. The downturn becomes, in a small way, something to welcome rather than dread. This reframe is genuinely useful for building the long-term investing mindset that wealth creation requires.

Building the habit of investing

There's also something valuable about the act of investing regularly. Consistent investing — even in modest amounts — builds financial confidence and reinforces your identity as someone who invests. Over years and decades, that habit compounds alongside your returns.

ASIC's MoneySmart resources emphasise that getting started and staying consistent matters more than perfect timing or finding the optimal strategy. Dollar cost averaging makes both of those things easier.

A few things to keep in mind

Dollar cost averaging is not a guarantee of profit, and it doesn't protect you from losses in a sustained market decline. If the market falls and stays down, you'll accumulate units at progressively lower prices — which sounds good, but only pays off if prices eventually recover. DCA works best as a long-term strategy, measured in years or decades rather than months.
