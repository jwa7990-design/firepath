Balanced funds explained — a bit of everything, less risk

Part of the FirePath Learning Lab · Educational only, not financial advice · Browse all topics →

What is a balanced fund?

A balanced fund is a type of investment that holds a mix of different asset classes — some focused on growth, others focused on stability. Rather than putting all your money into shares or all into bonds, a balanced fund spreads it across both, giving you a bit of everything in a single investment.

The idea is straightforward: growth assets (like shares and property) aim to build wealth over time, while defensive assets (like bonds and cash) act as a cushion when markets turn rough. Blend the two together, and you get something that sits in the middle — not as exciting as a pure shares fund in a bull market, but far less gut-wrenching when things go south.

Growth assets vs defensive assets

To understand balanced funds, it helps to understand what goes into them:

  • Growth assets include Australian and international shares, listed property trusts (REITs), and infrastructure. They tend to deliver higher returns over the long run but can swing dramatically in value — sometimes losing 20–30% in a bad year.
  • Defensive assets include government and corporate bonds, cash, and fixed interest investments. They offer more predictable, modest returns and help soften the blow when share markets fall.

ASIC's MoneySmart website classifies investment risk by asset mix. A "balanced" fund typically holds 60–76% in growth assets, while anything above that starts to edge into "growth" territory.

The typical 60/40 and 70/30 split

The most common structure you'll encounter is the 60/40 split — 60% growth assets and 40% defensive assets. This has been a cornerstone of portfolio construction for decades, and it remains a widely used benchmark for balanced investing globally.

In Australia, many superannuation funds label their balanced options with a 70/30 split — 70% growth, 30% defensive. This is actually considered "balanced" within the Australian super system, partly because Australians tend to have longer investment horizons through compulsory super contributions under the Superannuation Guarantee.

The split matters because it directly influences both your expected returns and your expected volatility. A 70/30 fund will generally outperform a 60/40 fund over a decade, but it will also fall further in a sharp market downturn. Neither is objectively better — it comes down to your personal situation.

Balanced options inside superannuation

For most Australians, the first place they encounter a balanced fund is inside their super account. The major industry and retail super funds — including those regulated by APRA (the Australian Prudential Regulation Authority) — all offer a balanced or similar option as their default or near-default choice.

When you join an employer's default super fund and don't actively choose an investment option, there's a good chance your money lands in something resembling a balanced fund. Under the MySuper framework, default options must meet certain diversification and risk requirements designed to protect members who haven't made an active choice.

What's actually inside a super balanced option?

Australian super fund balanced options typically invest across:

  • Australian shares (ASX-listed companies)
  • International shares (US, European, emerging markets)
  • Australian and global listed property
  • Infrastructure (toll roads, airports, utilities)
  • Australian and international fixed interest (bonds)
  • Cash and short-term money market instruments
  • Some larger funds also include unlisted (private) assets such as private equity or direct property

The inclusion of unlisted assets is more common in industry funds and can smooth out reported returns — but it also reduces transparency, since private assets aren't repriced daily like shares are.

The ATO's super comparison tool and ASIC's MoneySmart super comparison resources allow you to compare fund performance, fees, and investment options — useful if you're reviewing your current balanced option.

Balanced managed funds vs balanced ETFs

Outside of super, you can access balanced strategies through two main vehicles: managed funds and exchange-traded funds (ETFs).

Managed funds

A managed fund pools money from multiple investors and is run by a professional fund manager. With a balanced managed fund, the manager actively decides how to allocate between growth and defensive assets, and may adjust that mix as market conditions change. You typically invest directly with the fund manager or through a platform, and units are priced once a day.

Managed funds often come with higher fees — management expense ratios (MERs) of 0.5% to 1.5% per year are common — to pay for the active management involved.

Balanced ETFs

Balanced ETFs trade on the ASX like shares and typically follow a rules-based approach to maintaining a target asset allocation. Several providers offer diversified ETFs with built-in rebalancing across growth and defensive assets. Because they're largely passive (tracking indexes rather than relying on a manager's picks), fees tend to be significantly lower — often 0.17% to 0.35% per year.

The trade-off is that balanced ETFs don't adapt dynamically to market conditions the way an active manager might — though the evidence for active management consistently outperforming passive approaches over the long term is mixed at best.

The volatility trade-off

Here's the honest truth about balanced funds: you are intentionally giving up some return potential in exchange for a smoother ride.

Over the past 20 years, a portfolio of 100% Australian and international shares would have significantly outperformed a 60/40 balanced fund in terms of raw returns. But that same all-shares portfolio would also have fallen approximately 40–50% during the Global Financial Crisis and again sharply in early 2020. A balanced fund would have fallen considerably less in both cases — often 15–25% — and recovered faster.

When the RBA raises interest rates sharply (as it did from 2022 to 2023), bond prices fall, which can temporarily hurt the defensive side of a balanced fund too. This is worth understanding — defensive doesn't mean immune to losses, just typically less severe than shares alone.

ASIC's MoneySmart risk profile tool can help you work out whether the volatility of a balanced fund matches your personal risk tolerance and investment timeframe.

Who are balanced funds suited to?

Balanced funds aren't right for everyone, but they're well-suited to certain types of investors:

  • Shorter investment timeframes: If you're investing for 5–10 years rather than 20+, a balanced fund reduces the risk of a sharp market fall devastating your portfolio right before you need the money.
  • Lower risk tolerance: Some investors genuinely lose sleep over market falls. If seeing your balance drop 30% would cause you to sell at exactly the wrong time, a balanced fund's smoother journey has real behavioural value.
  • Retirees or near-retirees: Those drawing down on savings need more stability than someone still in the accumulation phase. Many Australians transition their super toward a balanced option as they approach retirement age.
  • Investors wanting simplicity: A single balanced fund handles diversification and rebalancing for you, which suits investors who don't want to manage multiple asset class exposures themselves.

Costs and fees — what to watch

Fees matter enormously over time. A difference of 0.5% per year in fees might sound trivial, but compounded over 20 or 30 years, it can represent tens of thousands of dollars in your final balance.
