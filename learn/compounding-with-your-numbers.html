Why Abstract Percentages Don't Tell the Full Story

Most explanations of compounding start with "imagine you invest $10,000 at 7% for 30 years." And while the maths is correct, it rarely lands emotionally. Real investing happens in weekly transfers, irregular lump sums, employer super contributions, and fee deductions — not clean textbook examples.

So let's do this properly. Real Australian amounts, realistic return assumptions, and the kind of scenarios that reflect how people actually build wealth here.

For context, the ASX has returned roughly 9–10% per year over the long run (including dividends), but after inflation and fees, a real-world planning figure of 7% per annum is commonly used by Australian financial planners for diversified growth portfolios. We'll use that as our base case, with variations.

Scenario 1: $200 Per Week — Starting at 25 vs 35

This is the most important comparison in personal finance. Ten years doesn't sound like much. The numbers say otherwise.

Assume someone invests $200 per week ($10,400 per year) into a diversified index fund returning 7% per annum, compounded annually. Here's what they accumulate by age 65:

Start Age Years Investing Total Contributed Final Portfolio Value Growth (Compounding)
Age 25 40 years $416,000 $2,198,000 $1,782,000
Age 35 30 years $312,000 $1,020,000 $708,000

The person who starts at 25 contributes only $104,000 more than the person who starts at 35. But they end up with $1,178,000 more. That gap — more than a million dollars — is entirely the product of time and compounding. The money that was invested in the 25-year-old's first decade does the heaviest lifting because it has the longest runway to grow.

The first decade of investing is worth more than the last three decades combined. Every year you delay is not just a year of lost contributions — it's a year of lost compounding on every future dollar you invest.

Scenario 2: $500 Per Week at Different Return Rates

Return rate matters — but perhaps less than people assume in the short term, and far more than people assume over long periods. Here's what $500 per week ($26,000 per year) looks like over 30 years at different annual return rates:

Annual Return Total Contributed Final Portfolio Value Equivalent Real Asset
4% (conservative/bonds) $780,000 $1,501,000 Comfortable but modest
7% (balanced growth) $780,000 $2,616,000 Strong retirement base
9% (high growth/equities) $780,000 $3,996,000 Significant wealth

The difference between 4% and 7% is $1.1 million on the same contributions. This is why asset allocation — how you split your money between growth assets like shares and defensive assets like bonds or cash — is one of the most consequential financial decisions you make. ASIC's MoneySmart website has tools to help understand what different asset allocations have historically returned.

The Hidden Wealth Destroyer: Fees

Fees don't feel dramatic. One percent sounds almost meaningless. Over decades, it is anything but.

Take $500,000 invested for 20 years at 7% gross return. Here's the impact of different annual fee levels on your ending balance:

Annual Fee Net Return Ending Balance Cost of Fees
0.10% (low-cost index fund) 6.90% $1,933,000 —
0.85% (mid-range managed fund) 6.15% $1,688,000 $245,000 lost
1.50% (active/adviser platform) 5.50% $1,497,000 $436,000 lost

A 1.4% fee difference costs you $436,000 over 20 years on a $500,000 starting balance. Fees compound in reverse — every dollar paid in fees is a dollar that never compounds for you. The ATO's superannuation comparison tools and ASIC's MoneySmart fee calculator both help visualise this effect on your super specifically.

Fees are the one guaranteed negative return in your portfolio. Unlike market returns, which are uncertain, fees are certain. Minimising them is one of the few things entirely within your control.

Lump Sum vs Regular Contributions

A common question: is it better to invest a lump sum immediately, or spread it over time? The honest answer depends on your psychology and market timing concerns, but mathematically, lump sum investing generally wins when markets trend upward over time.

Consider $50,000 to invest, with markets returning 7% annually:

Strategy After 10 Years After 20 Years
Lump sum invested immediately $98,358 $193,484
Spread equally over 12 months (~$4,167/month) $94,872 $186,622
