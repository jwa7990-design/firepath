What diversification actually means — don't put all your eggs in one basket

Part of the FirePath Learning Lab · Educational only, not financial advice · Browse all topics →

The Core Idea: Don't Bet Everything on One Outcome

Imagine you run a market stall selling only umbrellas. On rainy days, business is brilliant. On sunny days, you sell nothing. Now imagine the stall next to you sells sunscreen. Together, you'd cover both outcomes — one of you is always doing well, and the combined income is far more stable than either stall alone.

That's diversification in a nutshell. Instead of putting all your money into one investment and hoping it goes up, you spread it across different types of assets, industries, and regions. When one part of your portfolio struggles, another part may hold steady or even grow. The goal isn't to maximise every dollar of upside — it's to smooth out the ride and protect yourself from catastrophic losses.

The key insight: Diversification doesn't eliminate risk entirely. It reduces the risk that any single bad event wipes out your entire investment. You're trading the chance of a massive win for the security of a more predictable outcome.

Understanding Asset Classes

The first layer of diversification is spreading money across different asset classes — broad categories of investment that each behave differently under various economic conditions.

Shares (Equities)

When you buy shares, you own a small piece of a company. Shares listed on the ASX (Australian Securities Exchange) include household names like Commonwealth Bank, BHP, and Woolworths. Shares tend to offer higher long-term returns but can fall sharply during recessions or market panics. They're considered higher risk, higher reward.

Bonds (Fixed Income)

Bonds are essentially loans you make to governments or companies, which pay you interest over time. Australian government bonds, for example, are considered very low risk because the Australian government is unlikely to default. When share markets fall, bonds often hold their value or rise, making them a useful counterbalance. The RBA (Reserve Bank of Australia) sets interest rates that directly influence bond returns.

Property

Australians have a deep cultural affinity with property investment, and it genuinely has a place in a diversified portfolio. Beyond direct property ownership, you can access property markets through REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts) — funds that own commercial properties and are listed on the ASX. Property tends to move independently of shares, though it's not immune to economic downturns.

Cash

Cash — held in savings accounts, term deposits, or similar products — is the most stable asset class. It won't lose value overnight, but it also barely grows after inflation. A cash allocation in your portfolio provides stability and liquidity (meaning you can access it quickly), but holding too much cash long-term can quietly erode your purchasing power.

Your superannuation fund almost certainly already diversifies across these asset classes. Check your super fund's investment options — most default "balanced" or "growth" options spread contributions across shares, bonds, property, and cash automatically.

Geographic Diversification: Australia vs the World

Australia is a remarkable country, but its share market represents only about 2% of the global stock market. If you only invest in Australian shares, you're missing 98% of the world's publicly listed companies — including global giants in technology, healthcare, and consumer goods that simply don't exist on the ASX in meaningful scale.

Investing only in Australia also concentrates your risk in a single economy. If Australia enters a recession, is hit by falling commodity prices (our market is heavily weighted towards mining and banking), or faces a housing crisis, a purely domestic portfolio suffers badly. Adding international shares — across the US, Europe, Asia, and emerging markets — means your investments aren't all tied to one country's fortunes.

There are tax considerations with international investments that domestic ones don't have, and ASIC (Australian Securities and Investments Commission) regulates the disclosure requirements for international funds sold to Australian investors. The ATO (Australian Taxation Office) also has specific rules around foreign income, currency gains, and tax treaty benefits, so it's worth understanding the tax treatment of any international exposure you take on.

Sector Diversification Within Shares

Even within Australian shares, there's another layer of diversification to consider: sectors. The ASX is heavily dominated by financials (the big four banks) and materials (mining companies like BHP and Rio Tinto). If you simply buy "Australian shares" without thinking, you may end up very exposed to those two sectors.

A well-diversified share portfolio spreads exposure across sectors including:

  • Financials — banks, insurance companies, financial services
  • Materials — mining, metals, and resources
  • Healthcare — hospitals, pharmaceuticals, medical devices
  • Technology — software, IT services (more limited on the ASX, stronger globally)
  • Consumer staples — supermarkets, food producers, everyday goods
  • Energy — oil, gas, and increasingly renewables
  • Real estate and infrastructure — property trusts, toll roads, utilities

Different sectors thrive in different conditions. During economic booms, technology and consumer discretionary companies often surge. During downturns, consumer staples and healthcare tend to hold up better because people still buy groceries and need medical care regardless of what the economy is doing.

How a Single Index Fund Can Do Most of the Work

Here's where things get genuinely practical. You don't need to research dozens of individual companies to achieve solid diversification. A single index fund or ETF (Exchange Traded Fund) can give you instant, broad diversification in one purchase.

An index fund simply tracks a market index — a pre-defined list of companies. For example, a fund tracking the ASX 200 automatically holds shares in Australia's 200 largest listed companies across all sectors. A fund tracking a global index might hold thousands of companies across dozens of countries. Because these funds follow an index rather than relying on a fund manager to pick winners, they typically have very low fees.

A single globally diversified ETF can hold exposure to thousands of companies across multiple countries and sectors simultaneously. For many Australian investors — particularly those starting out — one or two well-chosen index funds may provide more genuine diversification than a complex portfolio of individually selected stocks.

Diversification vs Just Owning Lots of Things

This is one of the most important distinctions to understand. Owning many investments doesn't automatically mean you're diversified.

Consider these examples of false diversification:

  • Owning shares in ten different Australian mining companies — you have ten investments, but they'll all likely fall together if iron ore prices drop
  • Holding five different managed funds that all track the same index — you're paying five sets of fees for essentially identical exposure
  • Owning three investment properties all in the same suburb — property prices in that suburb move together

True diversification means owning assets that are not correlated — that is, assets that don't all move in the same direction at the same time for the same reasons. The goal is a portfolio where different pieces respond differently to economic events.
