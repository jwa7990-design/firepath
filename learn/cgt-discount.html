The 50% CGT discount, explained

If you sell shares or ETFs for more than you paid, you'll generally owe capital gains tax on the profit — but how long you held the asset before selling changes your tax bill significantly.

Capital gains tax (CGT) isn't a separate tax with its own rate — it's just your regular income tax, applied to the profit from selling an investment. The "gain" gets added to your taxable income for that year and taxed at your marginal rate. The discount is what determines how much of that gain actually counts.

The core rule

If you're an individual and you've held an asset for more than 12 months before selling it, only 50% of the capital gain is included in your taxable income. Sell within 12 months, and the full gain is taxable.

That's the entire mechanic — but the impact on your actual tax bill can be substantial, especially at higher marginal rates.

Worked example
You bought $20,000 of an ETF and sell it 18 months later for $28,000 — an $8,000 gain. Because you held it over 12 months, only $4,000 is taxable (half the gain). At a 32% marginal tax rate, that's roughly $1,280 in tax. If you'd sold at the 11-month mark instead, the full $8,000 gain would be taxable — around $2,560 in tax at the same rate. Same profit, roughly double the tax bill, purely because of timing.

What the discount does and doesn't apply to

Individuals (held >12 months)50% discount
Trusts (held >12 months)50% discount
Superannuation funds (accumulation)33% discount
CompaniesNo discount

Inside super, capital gains are taxed differently again — assets held over 12 months inside an accumulation account get a one-third discount, applied against the fund's 15% tax rate, which works out to an effective rate of around 10% on the gain.

Capital losses offset gains — but not income

If you've sold something else at a loss in the same year, that loss can offset a capital gain before the discount is applied — but capital losses can only offset capital gains, not your regular income. Unused capital losses carry forward indefinitely to offset gains in future years.

The order of operations matters

Losses are applied against the full (undiscounted) gain first, and only the net amount gets the 50% discount applied. This means realising a loss and a gain in the same year is generally more tax-efficient than spreading them across two separate years, if you have flexibility over timing.

The 12-month clock starts at purchase, not settlement

For shares and ETFs, the holding period is generally measured from the contract date (when you bought), not the settlement date. If you're close to the 12-month mark and deciding whether to sell now or wait a few weeks, it's worth checking the exact purchase date rather than assuming.
