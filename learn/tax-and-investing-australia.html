Tax and investing basics in Australia — what every investor needs to know

Part of the FirePath Learning Lab · Educational only, not financial advice · Browse all topics →

Why tax matters for investors

Investing is one of the most effective ways to build long-term wealth, but the returns you see on paper aren't always the returns you keep. Tax is one of the biggest real costs of investing, yet it's one that many Australian investors don't fully understand until they receive a surprise bill from the Australian Taxation Office (ATO). Getting a basic grasp of how investment income is taxed — and how to invest in a tax-efficient way — can make a meaningful difference to your outcomes over time.

This article covers the core tax concepts every Australian investor should understand. It's educational information, not personal tax advice — for guidance specific to your situation, always speak with a registered tax professional.

Capital Gains Tax (CGT) — selling investments

When you sell an investment for more than you paid for it, the profit is called a capital gain. In Australia, capital gains are taxed as part of your ordinary income — they're added to your taxable income for that financial year and taxed at your marginal rate.

How CGT works in practice

Say you bought shares on the ASX for $5,000 and sold them later for $8,000. Your capital gain is $3,000. That $3,000 gets added to your other income — salary, freelance work, interest, dividends — and you pay tax on the total at your marginal rate. If you're in the 32.5% tax bracket, you'd owe around $975 in tax on that gain.

The 12-month CGT discount

Here's one of the most valuable rules in Australian tax law for investors: if you hold an asset for at least 12 months before selling it, you're entitled to a 50% discount on your capital gain. Using the same example, your $3,000 gain would be halved to $1,500 before being added to your income — cutting your tax bill roughly in half.

Key insight: Holding an investment for more than 12 months before selling can halve the tax you pay on your profit. This is one reason long-term investing can be more tax-efficient than frequent trading.

When do you actually pay CGT?

CGT is triggered when you dispose of an asset — that means selling shares, selling an investment property, or transferring ownership. Simply holding an investment that has gone up in value doesn't trigger a tax event. You pay CGT when you lodge your annual tax return for the financial year in which the sale occurred.

How dividends are taxed

When Australian companies make a profit, they often distribute a portion to shareholders as dividends. These are a form of income, and like most income, they're taxable.

Dividends are added to your assessable income and taxed at your marginal rate. However, Australia has a system that prevents company profits from being taxed twice — and it's one of the most important concepts for local share investors to understand.

Franking credits explained simply

Australian companies pay corporate tax (currently 30% for large companies, 25% for smaller ones) on their profits before distributing dividends. The franking credit system — also called imputation credits — recognises that tax has already been paid and gives shareholders a credit for it.

When a company pays a fully franked dividend, it means the company has already paid 30% tax on those earnings. You receive the cash dividend plus a franking credit representing the tax already paid. When you lodge your tax return, you include both the dividend and the franking credit as income — but the credit is then used to offset what you owe.

  • If your marginal tax rate is higher than 30%, you pay the difference.
  • If your marginal tax rate is lower than 30%, you may receive a tax refund for the excess credit.
  • If your marginal rate is exactly 30%, the credit wipes out your tax liability on that dividend entirely.

Key insight: Franking credits are particularly valuable for retirees and low-income earners, who can receive refunds for credits that exceed their tax liability. For high-income earners, they still reduce — but don't eliminate — the tax on dividends.

Tax inside superannuation

Superannuation is Australia's retirement savings system, and its tax treatment is one of the main reasons financial educators consistently describe it as one of the most tax-effective investment structures available to Australians.

The 15% contributions tax

When your employer makes concessional contributions to your super (such as the compulsory Superannuation Guarantee, currently 11.5%), those contributions are taxed at just 15% inside the fund. If your marginal income tax rate is 32.5% or 45%, contributing to super means that money is taxed at a significantly lower rate. Salary sacrifice arrangements — where you voluntarily contribute pre-tax income to super — use the same principle.

Tax on investment earnings inside super

Investment returns earned within a super fund — capital gains, dividends, interest — are also taxed at a maximum of 15% during the accumulation phase (while you're still working and building your balance). Capital gains on assets held for more than 12 months receive a one-third discount inside super, effectively reducing that rate to 10%.

Tax-free after 60

Once you reach age 60 and access your super through a retirement income stream, your withdrawals are generally tax-free. Investment earnings in a retirement-phase account are also tax-free, up to a transfer balance cap set by the ATO (currently $1.9 million). This is a significant benefit for long-term retirement planning.

Tax-loss harvesting — using losses strategically

Tax-loss harvesting is the practice of selling investments that are sitting at a loss in order to offset capital gains you've made elsewhere. Because capital losses can be used to reduce your overall capital gains, this strategy can lower your tax bill in a given year.

For example, if you made a $5,000 capital gain on one parcel of shares but have another investment sitting at a $2,000 loss, selling the losing investment crystallises that loss and reduces your net capital gain to $3,000 — meaning less tax to pay.

  • Capital losses can only be used to offset capital gains, not ordinary income.
  • Unused capital losses can be carried forward to future tax years indefinitely.
  • Be cautious of the wash sale risk — the ATO looks unfavourably on selling an asset purely to crystallise a loss and immediately buying it back to maintain the position. This may be treated as a scheme to avoid tax.

Tax avoidance versus tax evasion

These two terms are often confused, but they have very different legal meanings — and consequences.

Tax avoidance refers to legally arranging your financial affairs to minimise the tax you pay. Holding investments for more than 12 months to access the CGT discount, contributing to super to benefit from lower tax rates, or using franking credits are all legitimate forms of tax minimisation. The ATO accepts — and in some cases actively encourages — these strategies.

Tax evasion, on the other hand, is illegal. It involves deliberately concealing income, falsifying records, or lying to the ATO to reduce your tax obligation. Evasion can result in serious penalties, prosecution, and imprisonment. ASIC and the ATO have significant powers to investigate and pursue tax evasion.
