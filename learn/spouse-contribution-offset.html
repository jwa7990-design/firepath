🧾 Tax & Strategy · Superannuation

Spouse contribution tax offset

If one partner earns significantly less than the other — maybe due to part-time work, career breaks, or caring responsibilities — contributing to their super can come with a small but genuine tax offset for the higher earner.

Superannuation balances between partners can end up wildly unequal over a working life, especially where one person has taken time out of the workforce. The spouse contribution offset is a small government incentive designed to encourage the higher-earning partner to help close that gap.

How the offset is calculated

If your spouse's income is $37,000 or less, you can claim an 18% tax offset on contributions you make to their super, up to $3,000 — a maximum offset of $540. The offset tapers down as their income rises, cutting out completely at $40,000.

The formula is: 18% of the lesser of ($3,000 minus every dollar your spouse earns over $37,000) or your actual contribution amount. In plain terms — contribute the full $3,000 while your spouse earns under $37,000, and you get the maximum $540 back as an offset on your own tax return.

Worked example
One partner earns $80,000, the other earns $34,000 working part-time. The higher earner contributes $5,000 into the lower earner's super as an after-tax contribution. Because the receiving partner's income is under $37,000, the contributing partner can claim the full $540 tax offset — calculated as 18% of the $3,000 that's eligible for the offset (the amount above $3,000 doesn't earn any extra offset, though it can still go in).

What "spouse" means here

This applies to both married and de facto relationships, including same-sex couples — you don't need to be legally married.

The conditions on the receiving side

The spouse receiving the contribution can't have exceeded their own non-concessional contributions cap ($130,000 for 2026–27), and their total super balance needs to be under the general transfer balance cap ($2.1 million) at the start of the year the contribution is made.

It's a contribution, not a deduction

Unlike salary sacrifice, this is an after-tax (non-concessional) contribution — you don't get a tax deduction for the contribution itself, and it's not taxed again going into your spouse's fund since it's already after-tax money. The offset is the entire benefit, claimed as item T3 on your own tax return.

Why it's worth considering beyond the dollar figure

The $540 maximum offset is modest on its own. Where this tends to matter more is as part of a broader strategy to balance super between partners — particularly relevant if one partner is approaching the point where unequal balances could affect things like Age Pension eligibility later, or if one partner's balance is closer to the $500,000 threshold that unlocks catch-up concessional contributions.
