🧾 Tax & Strategy · Superannuation

The government super co-contribution

If your income is under a certain threshold and you put your own after-tax money into super, the government adds 50 cents for every dollar — up to $500 a year. It's one of the few guaranteed returns available anywhere.

Most tax concessions in super work by reducing what you'd otherwise pay. This one is different — it's a genuine top-up. The government literally deposits extra money into your fund, on top of what you put in yourself, if you meet the income test.

How the numbers work

The government matches 50 cents for every $1 you contribute from your take-home pay (after-tax, non-concessional), up to a maximum co-contribution of $500 per year — which means contributing $1,000 yourself gets you the full amount.

For the 2026–27 financial year, the income thresholds are:

Full $500 co-contributionIncome under $49,293
Partial co-contribution$49,293 – $64,293
No co-contributionIncome $64,293 or more

Between the two thresholds, the maximum entitlement tapers down by 3.333 cents for every dollar your income sits above $49,293 — so it's a smooth reduction, not a cliff edge.

What actually counts as "income" here

It's not just your salary. The test uses your assessable income plus reportable fringe benefits plus reportable employer super contributions (including any salary sacrifice), minus allowable business deductions. This trips people up sometimes — someone who salary sacrifices heavily might have a lower take-home number but a higher figure for this specific test.

Eligibility beyond the income test

A few other conditions need to be met: at least 10% of your total income needs to come from employment or running a business (investment income like dividends and rent doesn't count toward this), you need to be under 71 at the end of the financial year, and your total super balance needs to be under the general transfer balance cap ($2.1 million for 2026–27) as of the previous 30 June.

Worked example
Someone earning $45,000 a year decides to contribute $1,000 from their savings into super. Because their income is under the $49,293 lower threshold, they get the full $500 co-contribution — a guaranteed 50% return, paid straight into their fund, with no application needed. The ATO checks eligibility automatically once the tax return is lodged.

You don't apply for it

There's no form. The ATO cross-checks your contribution against your lodged tax return and pays the co-contribution directly into whichever fund received your personal contribution — usually between November and January following the end of the financial year. The only actions required are making the contribution before 30 June and making sure your fund has your tax file number.

Worth noting: this is separate from the Low Income Super Tax Offset (LISTO), which works differently and can apply in the same year if you're eligible for both.
