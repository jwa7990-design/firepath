What is FIRE?

FIRE stands for Financial Independence, Retire Early. It's a movement built around the idea that with intentional saving, smart investing, and some lifestyle design, you don't have to wait until your mid-60s to stop working. The goal is to accumulate enough invested assets that your investment returns can cover your living expenses indefinitely — giving you the freedom to work on your own terms, or not at all.

FIRE isn't a single rigid strategy. It's an umbrella term that covers a wide spectrum of approaches, from extreme frugality to comfortable semi-retirement. Understanding the vocabulary used in FIRE communities will help you cut through the noise and find the path that makes sense for your own situation.

The Core FIRE Concepts

FI — Financial Independence

Financial Independence (FI) means your investments generate enough passive income to cover your living expenses without needing to work. Many people pursue FI even if they have no intention of retiring early — they simply want the security and freedom that comes from not depending on a pay cheque.

RE — Retire Early

Retire Early (RE) is the optional second half of FIRE. For some people, reaching FI means leaving traditional employment immediately. For others, it means shifting to part-time work, freelancing, or pursuing passion projects. "Retirement" in the FIRE context rarely means sitting on a beach doing nothing — it usually means having the freedom to choose how you spend your time.

Your FI Number (or Freedom Number)

Your FI number — sometimes called your freedom number — is the total amount of invested assets you need to be considered financially independent. It's calculated based on your annual expenses and the withdrawal rate you plan to use. Once your investment portfolio hits this number, you've reached FIRE.

A simple way to estimate your FI number: multiply your expected annual expenses by 25. So if you plan to spend $60,000 per year in retirement, your FI number is approximately $1,500,000.

The 4% Rule and Safe Withdrawal Rate (SWR)

The 4% rule comes from the Trinity Study, a piece of US research suggesting that retirees can withdraw 4% of their portfolio annually with a very high probability of the money lasting at least 30 years. The broader concept is called the Safe Withdrawal Rate (SWR) — the maximum percentage you can withdraw each year without running out of money over a given retirement period.

Australian investors should approach the 4% rule with some nuance. Your retirement could span 40–50 years if you retire early, Australian market returns differ from US market history, and your superannuation access rules (more on those below) change the maths significantly. Many Australian FIRE planners use a more conservative SWR of 3% to 3.5% for very long retirements.

FIRE Variations — Finding Your Flavour

Lean FIRE

Lean FIRE means reaching financial independence on a minimal budget — typically below $40,000 per year for a single person in Australia. It usually requires significant lifestyle downsizing and leaves little room for unexpected expenses. It's achievable sooner, but demands ongoing frugality.

Fat FIRE

Fat FIRE is financial independence with a generous lifestyle budget — often considered $100,000 or more per year. It requires a much larger portfolio but allows you to maintain or even upgrade your current standard of living in retirement.

Barista FIRE

Barista FIRE (the name comes from the idea of taking a low-stress part-time job) means your investment portfolio covers most of your expenses, but you still earn a small income to cover the gap or extras. This approach suits people who want to leave demanding careers but aren't quite ready to stop working entirely.

Coast FIRE

Coast FIRE means you've saved and invested enough that — without adding another dollar — your portfolio will grow to fund a traditional retirement by the time you reach a conventional retirement age. You can "coast" from here, covering day-to-day expenses with your income without needing to aggressively save. This is particularly relevant in Australia given the long compounding runway available through superannuation.

Essential Investing Terms

ETF — Exchange Traded Fund

An Exchange Traded Fund (ETF) is a basket of assets — often hundreds of shares — that trades on a stock exchange like the ASX (Australian Securities Exchange). ETFs are popular with FIRE investors because they offer instant diversification at a low cost. You can buy a single ETF and gain exposure to the entire Australian share market, global markets, or specific sectors.

Index Fund

An index fund tracks a specific market index — like the ASX 200 (Australia's top 200 companies) or the S&P 500 (the 500 largest US companies). Rather than trying to beat the market, index funds simply replicate it. Decades of evidence suggest most actively managed funds underperform their benchmark index over the long term, which is why index funds are a cornerstone of most FIRE strategies.

Compounding

Compounding is the process where your investment returns generate their own returns over time. Reinvested dividends buy more shares, which pay more dividends, which buy even more shares. The longer your money is invested, the more dramatic the compounding effect becomes — which is why starting early is so powerful in the FIRE journey.

Dollar Cost Averaging (DCA)

Dollar cost averaging means investing a fixed dollar amount at regular intervals — say, $500 every fortnight — regardless of whether markets are up or down. When prices are high, your $500 buys fewer units. When prices are low, it buys more. Over time, this smooths out your average purchase price and removes the temptation to time the market.

Asset Allocation and Diversification

Asset allocation refers to how you spread your investments across different asset classes — shares, property, bonds, cash, and so on. Diversification means spreading risk within and across those asset classes so that a single company collapse or market downturn doesn't devastate your entire portfolio. ASIC's MoneySmart website offers plain-language guidance on both concepts for Australian investors.

Rebalancing

Rebalancing is the process of periodically reviewing your portfolio and adjusting it back to your target asset allocation. If Australian shares have had a strong run and now make up 70% of your portfolio when you wanted 60%, rebalancing means selling some and buying other assets to restore your intended split.

Australian-Specific Terms

Superannuation

Superannuation (super) is Australia's compulsory retirement savings system, overseen by the ATO (Australian Taxation Office). Employers are currently required to contribute a percentage of your salary into a super fund on your behalf (the rate is set to reach 12% by 2025). Super is one of the most tax-effective investment vehicles available to Australians, but access is restricted until you reach your preservation age.

Preservation Age

Your preservation age is the earliest age at which you can access your superannuation. For most Australians born after 1 July 1964, this is age 60. This restriction is a critical consideration in FIRE planning — if you retire at 40, you'll need to fund your lifestyle from outside-super investments for potentially two decades before you can access your super balance.
