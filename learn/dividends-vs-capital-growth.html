Dividends vs capital growth — income now or wealth later?

Part of the FirePath Learning Lab · Educational only, not financial advice · Browse all topics →

Two Paths to Financial Independence

When building wealth, Australian investors eventually face a fundamental question: should you focus on investments that pay regular income, or those that grow in value over time? This isn't just an academic debate — the answer shapes which assets you buy, how you're taxed, and how you eventually fund your lifestyle in retirement or financial independence.

The good news is that both approaches can work. The better news is that understanding the mechanics of each helps you make a genuinely informed choice rather than following trends or forum opinions.

What Are Dividends?

A dividend is a cash payment that a company makes to its shareholders, typically from its profits. When you own shares in a company like Commonwealth Bank or BHP, or units in an exchange-traded fund (ETF) that holds shares, you may receive dividends periodically — often twice a year for ASX-listed companies.

The dividend yield expresses that income as a percentage of the investment's price. If a share trades at $40 and pays $2 in annual dividends, the yield is 5%. This makes it easy to compare income across different investments.

The Australian Advantage: Franking Credits

Australia has one of the most investor-friendly dividend tax systems in the world, thanks to franking credits (also called imputation credits). Here's how it works in plain terms.

When an Australian company earns a profit, it pays company tax — currently 30% for large companies. If it then distributes that profit as a dividend, shareholders could end up being taxed twice on the same money. The franking credit system prevents this by attaching a tax credit to the dividend, representing the tax already paid by the company.

For example, if you receive a $700 fully franked dividend, it comes with a $300 franking credit (reflecting 30% company tax paid). Your assessable income is $1,000, but you get a $300 credit against your tax bill. If your marginal tax rate is lower than 30%, the ATO may even refund the difference — a genuine cash benefit that doesn't exist in most other countries.

Key insight: For retirees or low-income earners whose marginal tax rate falls below 30%, fully franked dividends can generate cash tax refunds from the ATO — effectively boosting the real yield beyond the stated figure.

This makes Australian dividend investing particularly compelling for people in low tax brackets, including self-funded retirees drawing from superannuation or a self-managed super fund (SMSF).

Capital Growth: Building Wealth Through Price Appreciation

Capital growth refers to an increase in the value of your investment over time. A share you bought for $10 that is now worth $18 has delivered $8 in capital growth. You don't receive that gain as income — it only becomes real money when you sell.

Many growth-oriented companies, particularly in technology, reinvest profits back into the business rather than paying dividends. Investors accept no income now in exchange for potentially higher price appreciation later.

Index funds and broad ETFs on the ASX often blend both — they hold dividend-paying companies and grow in value over time. But the emphasis matters: a high-yield dividend ETF behaves quite differently from a broad total market fund or a growth-tilted fund.

The Total Return Approach

Many financial planners and evidence-based investors use a total return framework, which treats dividends and capital growth as interchangeable. The logic is straightforward: a dollar received as a dividend and a dollar gained through price appreciation are both a dollar of wealth created. The source shouldn't dictate the strategy.

Under this approach, investors don't seek high-yield assets specifically. Instead, they invest in the most efficient, well-diversified portfolio available, then sell a portion of units or shares periodically to fund living expenses when needed. This is sometimes called manufacturing your own dividend.

Why the Maths Often Favours Total Return in Accumulation

During your working years — the accumulation phase — there are real tax costs to prioritising dividends over growth. Every dividend you receive outside of superannuation is added to your taxable income in that financial year, whether you needed the cash or not. If you're earning a salary and receiving dividends on top, those dividends are taxed at your marginal rate, which could be 34.5%, 39%, or even 47% including the Medicare levy.

Capital growth, by contrast, is only taxed when you sell — and only at your marginal rate, minus the 50% capital gains tax (CGT) discount if you've held the asset for more than 12 months. This means you have more control over when you realise a gain and can potentially manage it into a lower-income year.

Key insight: In accumulation, a high-income earner receiving $10,000 in dividends may keep only $5,300 after tax. The same $10,000 in unrealised capital growth stays fully invested, compounding for years before any tax is triggered.

Dividend Reinvestment Plans (DRPs)

Many ASX-listed companies and funds offer a Dividend Reinvestment Plan (DRP), which automatically uses your dividend payment to purchase additional shares rather than depositing cash. This is a useful middle ground — you get the compounding effect without needing to manually reinvest, though you still pay tax on the dividend income received, even if you didn't touch the cash.

Why Some FIRE Investors Love Dividends

The FIRE movement (Financial Independence, Retire Early) has a significant contingent who prefer dividend-focused investing, and their reasons are psychologically and practically sound.

  • Predictability: Regular dividend income feels more like a salary. You don't need to sell anything to fund your life.
  • Behavioural ease: During a market crash, dividends often continue even when share prices fall sharply. This makes it easier to stay invested without panic-selling.
  • Sequence of returns: Retirees who rely on selling assets for income face sequence of returns risk — selling at depressed prices early in retirement can permanently damage a portfolio. Dividend income can reduce forced selling.
  • Simplicity in drawdown: Spending dividends without selling units removes the need to calculate and execute regular sell orders.

These are legitimate advantages, particularly in the decumulation phase — when you're drawing down rather than building up.

Tax Implications: A Side-by-Side View

Dividend Income (Outside Super)

  • Assessable income in the year received, regardless of whether you needed it
  • Franking credits offset tax, potentially generating refunds for low-income earners
  • No ability to defer tax timing

Capital Gains (Outside Super)

  • Only taxed when you sell
  • 50% CGT discount applies for assets held over 12 months
  • You control the timing, allowing strategic planning around income

Inside Superannuation

Both dividend income and capital gains are taxed at a maximum of 15% in the accumulation phase within super, and at 0% once you're in pension phase (subject to transfer balance cap rules). This dramatically changes the relative advantage of each approach — inside super, dividends become significantly more tax-efficient, which is one reason many SMSFs hold high-yielding Australian shares.
