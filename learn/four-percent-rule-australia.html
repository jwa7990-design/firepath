The 4% rule — where it comes from and does it work in Australia?

Part of the FirePath Learning Lab · Educational only, not financial advice · Browse all topics →

Where the 4% Rule Comes From

The 4% rule has become one of the most widely cited concepts in retirement planning, but most people who quote it have never read the original research. Understanding where it came from — and what it actually claimed — is essential before applying it to your own situation.

The rule originates from what is commonly called the Trinity Study, a 1998 paper published by three professors at Trinity University in Texas: Philip Cooley, Carl Hubbard, and Daniel Walz. Their paper, titled Retirement Savings: Choosing a Withdrawal Rate That Is Sustainable, analysed historical US market data to determine what percentage of a portfolio a retiree could withdraw each year without running out of money.

The researchers looked at rolling historical periods using US stock and bond market returns from 1926 onwards. They tested different withdrawal rates (from 3% to 12%) across different portfolio compositions (ranging from 100% stocks to 100% bonds) and asked a simple question: in how many historical scenarios did the portfolio survive the full retirement period?

Their conclusion was that a 4% annual withdrawal rate, applied to a portfolio of roughly 50–75% stocks and the remainder in bonds, had a very high success rate across historical 30-year retirement periods. The 4% figure was adjusted for inflation each year — meaning you'd withdraw 4% in year one, then increase that dollar amount with inflation in subsequent years to maintain purchasing power.

The original finding: A retiree withdrawing 4% of their initial portfolio value in year one, then adjusting for inflation annually, had approximately a 95% historical success rate across 30-year periods using US market data.

What the 4% Rule Actually Says

There is a lot of misunderstanding about what the rule does and does not claim. It is worth being precise.

  • The 4% is based on your initial portfolio value, not recalculated each year. If you retire with $1,000,000, you withdraw $40,000 in year one. If inflation is 3%, you withdraw $41,200 in year two — regardless of whether your portfolio has grown or shrunk.
  • It assumes a 30-year retirement horizon. The Trinity Study was designed around a traditional retirement age (mid-60s) with a roughly 30-year planning window.
  • It is a historical success rate, not a guarantee. It tells you how often this strategy would have worked in the past, not that it will work in the future.
  • It was based on US market data. US equities have historically been among the strongest performers globally. This matters enormously when applying the rule to Australian portfolios.

The Freedom Number: 25 Times Your Annual Spending

The 4% rule translates directly into a useful planning shortcut known as the freedom number or sometimes the "FI number" (from the financial independence community). The logic is simple algebra: if you can safely withdraw 4% of your portfolio each year, then you need a portfolio worth 25 times your annual spending to fund retirement indefinitely.

For example, if your household spends $60,000 per year, your freedom number would be $60,000 × 25 = $1,500,000. At a 3.5% withdrawal rate, that same spending level would require approximately $1,714,000. At 3%, it climbs to $2,000,000.

This is a genuinely useful mental model for goal-setting. Rather than thinking vaguely about "saving enough for retirement," the freedom number gives you a concrete target to work towards inside and outside of superannuation.

Why 40–50 Year Retirements Change Everything

The original Trinity Study was built around a 30-year timeframe. For someone retiring at 65 and planning to age 95, that is reasonable. But two groups of Australians face much longer retirements:

  • Early retirees — those pursuing financial independence in their 40s or 50s may need their portfolio to last 40–50 years.
  • Healthy Australians retiring at 60 — with average life expectancy for a 60-year-old Australian male now approaching 84, and females around 87, a 30-year plan may already be cutting it close.

Extended modelling (including updated work by Wade Pfau, a prominent US retirement researcher, and subsequent studies) consistently shows that success rates fall meaningfully when the retirement horizon extends beyond 30 years. A 4% withdrawal rate that succeeds in 95% of 30-year scenarios might succeed in only 80–85% of 50-year scenarios using the same data. That gap represents a meaningful risk of running out of money late in life — arguably the worst possible time for that to happen.

For Australians planning a retirement of 40 years or more, many researchers suggest a more conservative starting withdrawal rate of 3% to 3.5% provides substantially greater confidence of portfolio survival.

The Australian Context: Super, the Age Pension, and Market Differences

Superannuation Changes the Calculation

Australia's compulsory superannuation system is a significant structural advantage that the original Trinity Study did not account for. For most Australians, the bulk of retirement savings sits within the superannuation system, which carries meaningful tax advantages — earnings in the accumulation phase are taxed at just 15%, and in the retirement (pension) phase, investment earnings are generally tax-free according to current ATO rules.

This tax efficiency means an Australian retiree drawing from a superannuation account in pension phase keeps more of their investment returns than a comparable investor drawing from a taxable account. That is a genuine improvement on the Trinity Study's assumptions.

The Age Pension as a Safety Net

The Australian Age Pension provides a meaningful floor that does not exist in the same way in the United States. As of 2024, the full Age Pension for a single person provides over $27,000 per year (including supplements), with a couple receiving over $41,000 combined. For retirees who partially or fully exhaust their savings, this acts as a genuine safety net.

This has a powerful implication for withdrawal rate planning: an Australian retiree who draws down their portfolio more aggressively in early retirement, knowing the Age Pension will eventually provide baseline income, can justify a higher withdrawal rate than the Trinity Study's pure portfolio-based model suggests. This is sometimes called a "rising equity glidepath" strategy or simply a hybrid income approach.

Australian vs US Market Returns

One of the most important and under-discussed aspects of applying the 4% rule in Australia is that it was built on US market data — and the US has been an exceptional market by global historical standards. Research examining international market data, including work by Pfau and others, consistently finds that safe withdrawal rates are lower in most countries when using their own historical market data rather than US data.

The ASX has delivered strong long-term returns, particularly when franking credits (tax credits attached to dividends paid on Australian shares) are included. However, the Australian share market is heavily concentrated — the top 10 ASX stocks represent a substantial portion of the entire index, with a significant weighting towards banks and resources. This concentration risk, combined with Australia's smaller market relative to the US, suggests some caution is warranted when assuming US-derived withdrawal rates will translate perfectly.
