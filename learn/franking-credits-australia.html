Franking credits in Australia — a uniquely Australian tax advantage

Part of the FirePath Learning Lab · Educational only, not financial advice · Browse all topics →

What Are Franking Credits?

If you own shares in Australian companies, you've probably noticed something called "franking credits" appearing on your dividend statements. For many investors — especially beginners — these credits look like accounting noise. In reality, they can be worth thousands of dollars a year and represent one of the most investor-friendly features of the Australian tax system.

Franking credits (also called imputation credits) are a tax offset passed on to shareholders when an Australian company pays dividends. They exist because of a simple principle: company profits should only be taxed once. Without franking credits, those profits would be taxed twice — first when the company earns them, and again when shareholders receive them as dividends.

Why Does Australia Have This System?

Australia introduced dividend imputation in 1987 under the Hawke-Keating government, making it one of the first countries in the world to adopt the system at a national scale. The logic is straightforward.

When an Australian company earns a profit, it pays corporate tax to the Australian Taxation Office (ATO) — currently at a rate of 30% for large companies, or 25% for smaller base-rate entities. When that same company then distributes its after-tax profits to shareholders as a dividend, the shareholder would ordinarily pay income tax on the full dividend amount. Without any adjustment, the same dollar of profit has been taxed twice.

The imputation system fixes this by attaching a "credit" to the dividend — a record of the tax the company has already paid on your behalf. When you lodge your tax return, that credit offsets the income tax you owe on the dividend, so you're only paying tax once at your own marginal rate, not twice.

The core idea: A franking credit isn't a bonus payment — it's a record that tax has already been paid on your dividend income. The Australian tax system passes that prepaid tax back to you so you're not taxed twice on the same money.

How Franking Credits Appear on Your Dividend Statement

When you receive a dividend from an ASX-listed company, your dividend statement will typically show three key figures:

  • Cash dividend amount — the actual money deposited into your account
  • Franking credit amount — the tax already paid by the company on your behalf
  • Grossed-up dividend — the total of both, representing your true pre-tax income

For example, if you receive a $70 cash dividend with $30 in franking credits attached, your grossed-up dividend is $100. This reflects the fact that the company earned $100, paid $30 in tax, and distributed the remaining $70 to you.

Fully Franked vs Partially Franked Dividends

Not all dividends carry the full amount of franking credits. This depends on how much Australian corporate tax the company has paid on the relevant profits.

  • Fully franked dividends carry credits equal to the full 30% (or 25%) corporate tax rate. The most common scenario for established ASX companies like the major banks, supermarkets, and resource companies.
  • Partially franked dividends carry credits for only part of the tax rate — this happens when a company's profits include income that wasn't taxed at the full Australian corporate rate, such as overseas earnings.
  • Unfranked dividends carry no credits at all. This is typical for companies that pay no Australian corporate tax, operate primarily offshore, or have tax losses that shelter their income.

The franking percentage is always shown on your dividend statement, so you'll know exactly what you're receiving.

Claiming Franking Credits in Your Tax Return

The ATO makes claiming franking credits relatively straightforward, particularly if you use myTax or a registered tax agent. Here's how it works in practice:

  1. Your dividend statement shows the cash dividend and the franking credit amount
  2. You include the grossed-up dividend as income in your tax return
  3. You claim the franking credit as a tax offset against your tax liability
  4. If the offset exceeds your tax bill, the ATO refunds the difference

Many brokers and share registries (such as Computershare and Link Market Services) now pre-populate dividend information directly into the ATO's systems, which myTax can import automatically. Always cross-check this information against your own records.

Important: You must hold shares for at least 45 days (not counting the purchase or sale day) around the dividend record date to be eligible to claim franking credits. This is known as the holding period rule, and it exists to prevent people from buying shares just before a dividend and selling immediately after simply to collect credits.

Refundable Credits — The Benefit for Low-Income Investors

One of the most powerful features of Australia's imputation system is that franking credits are refundable. This means that if your franking credits exceed your total tax liability for the year, the ATO will send you a cash refund for the difference.

This makes franking credits particularly valuable for:

  • Retirees with income below the tax-free threshold ($18,200 for the 2024–25 year)
  • Low-income earners whose marginal tax rate is below the corporate tax rate
  • Investors in pension-phase superannuation (discussed below)

For example, a retired investor with $20,000 in dividend income and $8,000 in attached franking credits might have a tax liability of just $342 after applying the low income tax offset. The remaining $7,658 in franking credits would be refunded in full by the ATO.

Franking Credits and Superannuation

The interaction between franking credits and superannuation is where things become especially compelling for Australian investors planning for retirement.

Superannuation funds in accumulation phase pay 15% tax on investment earnings, which is below the 30% corporate tax rate on large companies. This means super funds often receive a partial refund of excess franking credits on their Australian share holdings.

Superannuation funds in pension phase pay zero tax on investment earnings. This means that for every dollar of franking credit received on Australian shares held within a pension-phase super fund, the full amount is refundable in cash from the ATO. For retirees drawing on a self-managed super fund (SMSF) or an industry or retail fund with a sufficiently large Australian share allocation, this can represent a meaningful and reliable additional income stream.

A pension-phase super fund holding a portfolio of fully franked Australian shares effectively receives a 30% tax bonus on every dividend — the corporate tax that was prepaid on its behalf comes back as a cash refund. This is a significant structural advantage of investing in Australian equities through superannuation.

Why International Shares Don't Have Franking Credits

Franking credits only arise from Australian corporate tax paid to the ATO. When you invest in overseas companies — whether directly through a US stock, a global ETF, or an international managed fund — the dividends you receive have been paid out of profits taxed in other countries. Those foreign tax systems have no obligation to pass credits through to Australian shareholders.
