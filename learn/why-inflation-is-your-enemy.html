Why inflation is your silent enemy — and what to do about it

Part of the FirePath Learning Lab · Educational only, not financial advice · Browse all topics →

What Is Inflation, and Why Should You Care?

Inflation is the gradual rise in the price of goods and services over time. When inflation is running at 3% per year, something that costs $100 today will cost $103 next year. That might sound modest, but compounded over decades, it quietly and relentlessly chips away at the real value of your money.

For everyday Australians, inflation shows up in the weekly grocery bill, electricity costs, and the price of a cup of coffee. But its most damaging effect isn't visible at the checkout — it happens silently inside your savings account, your superannuation balance, and your long-term financial plans.

How Australia Measures Inflation

The Consumer Price Index (CPI)

Australia's official measure of inflation is the Consumer Price Index (CPI), published quarterly by the Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS). The CPI tracks the price changes of a representative "basket" of goods and services that typical Australian households buy — things like food, housing, transport, healthcare, and education.

When you hear that "inflation rose to 4.1%," it means the prices in that basket are, on average, 4.1% higher than they were a year ago. It doesn't mean every single item went up by that amount — some went up more, some went up less — but it gives a useful overall picture of how far your dollar is stretching.

The RBA's Target Band

The Reserve Bank of Australia (RBA) — Australia's central bank — has a formal target of keeping inflation between 2% and 3% per year over time. This target exists because a small, predictable level of inflation is actually considered healthy for a growing economy. It encourages spending and investment rather than hoarding cash.

When inflation climbs well above 3%, as it did in 2022 and 2023 when Australian CPI peaked above 7%, the RBA typically raises interest rates to cool economic activity and bring prices back under control. When inflation falls below 2%, it may cut rates to stimulate growth.

Key insight: Even at the RBA's "ideal" midpoint of 2.5% inflation, the purchasing power of $1 today will fall to roughly $0.60 in 20 years. Inflation doesn't need to be extreme to cause serious damage to your wealth over time.

How Inflation Erodes Your Purchasing Power

Let's make this concrete. Suppose you have $100,000 sitting in cash today. Here's what inflation does to its real purchasing power at different rates:

  • At 2.5% inflation (RBA midpoint) over 20 years: your $100,000 would need to grow to approximately $163,900 just to maintain the same purchasing power. In real terms, if it hasn't grown, it's worth only around $61,000 in today's dollars.
  • At 3% inflation over 20 years: you'd need about $180,600 to match today's $100,000. Uninvested, your real purchasing power drops to roughly $55,000.
  • At 4% inflation (as seen in recent years) over 20 years: today's $100,000 becomes worth just $45,600 in real terms.

This is the silent erosion that most people don't feel day to day — but it accumulates into a devastating long-term wealth gap.

The Problem With Leaving Money in a Bank Account

Many Australians feel "safe" keeping large sums in savings accounts or term deposits. There's genuine value in liquidity and capital preservation — but there's a real cost that rarely gets discussed: the real return.

The real return is simply:

Real Return = Nominal Interest Rate − Inflation Rate

If your savings account pays 4.5% interest but inflation is running at 4%, your real return is just 0.5%. You're barely treading water. And in periods where savings rates sit at 1–2% (as they did for much of the 2010s) while inflation runs at 2.5–3%, your real return is negative — meaning you're losing purchasing power every single year, even while your balance nominally grows.

Key insight: A growing number on your bank statement can still represent shrinking wealth. Always ask: "What is my return after inflation?" That's the only number that tells you whether you're getting richer or poorer in real terms.

It's also worth noting that interest earned in a savings account is assessable income under Australian tax law — the Australian Taxation Office (ATO) will tax it at your marginal rate. So if you're in the 32.5% tax bracket and earning 4.5% interest, your after-tax nominal return is closer to 3%. Subtract inflation, and the real, after-tax return can easily be near zero or negative.

Investments That Have Historically Beaten Inflation

Australian and International Shares

Over long periods, shares (equities) have been one of the most reliable ways to outpace inflation. The ASX 200 — Australia's benchmark index of the 200 largest listed companies — has historically delivered total returns (capital growth plus dividends) of around 9–10% per year on average, well ahead of inflation.

Australian shares also carry a unique tax advantage: franking credits. Many ASX-listed companies pay dividends with attached franking credits, which represent company tax already paid. These credits can reduce your personal tax liability or generate a refund — effectively boosting your real, after-tax return.

Of course, shares come with volatility. Prices rise and fall, sometimes dramatically. But for investors with a long time horizon — say, 10 years or more — the historical evidence strongly favours equities as an inflation-beating asset class.

Property

Australian residential property has a well-documented history of long-term price growth, particularly in major cities. Beyond capital appreciation, rental income provides an ongoing return. Property also benefits from tax provisions like negative gearing and the capital gains tax (CGT) discount for assets held longer than 12 months — both of which can improve after-tax real returns.

However, property requires significant capital, carries transaction costs, and is illiquid. It's not the right tool for every investor, but for those who can access it, it has historically provided real returns above inflation over the long term.

Superannuation

Your super fund is one of the most powerful inflation-fighting tools available to Australians — and it's easy to overlook because the money feels locked away. Super invested in a diversified growth option typically holds a mix of Australian and international shares, property, and infrastructure. Over long time horizons, these assets have consistently delivered real returns above inflation.

The tax advantages of super — contributions taxed at just 15%, and investment earnings taxed at 15% during accumulation — mean your real, after-tax returns inside super are often significantly better than equivalent investments held in your own name. The Australian Prudential Regulation Authority (APRA) and ASIC both oversee the super system to ensure funds act in members' best interests.
