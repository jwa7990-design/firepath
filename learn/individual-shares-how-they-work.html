Individual shares — how they work and what you're actually buying

Part of the FirePath Learning Lab · Educational only, not financial advice · Browse all topics →

What You're Actually Buying

When you buy a share in a company, you're buying a small slice of ownership in that business. If a company has issued 100 million shares and you own 1,000 of them, you own one hundred-thousandth of the company — its assets, its future earnings, and yes, its debts and problems too.

This isn't just a number on a screen. As a shareholder, you have legal rights: the right to vote on major company decisions, the right to receive dividends if the company pays them, and in the event the company winds up, the right to a proportional share of whatever's left after creditors are paid. That last part matters — shareholders are last in line when things go wrong, which is why shares carry more risk than bonds or cash.

In Australia, shares in publicly listed companies are bought and sold through the Australian Securities Exchange (ASX), which is the marketplace that connects buyers and sellers. The ASX lists over 2,000 companies, from the big four banks and mining giants like BHP and Rio Tinto, all the way to small speculative explorers with a few million dollars in market value.

How the ASX Works

The ASX operates like an auction market. When you place an order through a broker — whether that's a full-service broker or an online platform like CommSec, Superhero, or Stake — your order enters the market and is matched with someone willing to sell at your price, or vice versa.

Trading on the ASX happens during market hours, generally 10am to 4pm AEST on weekdays, excluding public holidays. Prices update in real time as trades are made. Outside of these hours, you can place orders that will be executed when the market opens.

The ASX is regulated by ASIC (the Australian Securities and Investments Commission), which oversees market integrity, requires companies to disclose material information to all investors simultaneously, and investigates insider trading and market manipulation. This doesn't eliminate risk, but it does mean Australian investors have meaningful protections compared to many global markets.

What Drives Share Prices

Share prices move constantly, and understanding why helps you make better decisions — or at least avoid panicking at the wrong time.

Company earnings

The most fundamental driver of a share price is a company's earnings — how much profit it makes. When a company reports better profits than expected, its share price typically rises. When earnings disappoint, the price usually falls. This is why the ASX reporting seasons (February and August, when most companies release their results) can cause significant price movements.

Investor sentiment

Markets are made of people, and people are emotional. Fear, greed, and herd behaviour move prices in ways that have nothing to do with the underlying business. A perfectly healthy company can see its share price fall 20% during a broad market selloff, not because anything changed at the company, but because investors are rushing for the exit.

Macroeconomic factors

Interest rate decisions by the Reserve Bank of Australia (RBA), inflation data, unemployment figures, and global events all influence share prices. When interest rates rise, borrowing costs for companies increase and investors can get better returns from safer assets like bonds and savings accounts — both of which tend to push share prices down. When rates fall, the reverse often happens.

Share prices reflect expectations about the future, not just current reality. You're not buying what a company earned last year — you're buying what the market believes it will earn going forward.

Dividends and How They Work in Australia

Many Australian companies pay dividends — regular cash payments made to shareholders from company profits. These are typically paid twice a year and are expressed either as a dollar amount per share or as a yield (the dividend as a percentage of the share price).

Australia has a unique system called franking credits (also called imputation credits). When a company pays Australian tax on its profits and then distributes dividends, it can pass those tax credits on to shareholders. This means you may receive a tax credit alongside your dividend, reducing the tax you owe — or even generating a refund if you're a low-income earner. The ATO administers this system, and your annual tax return is where you claim franking credits.

For retirees and self-managed super funds (SMSFs), franked dividends can be particularly valuable. It's one reason Australian investors have historically favoured domestic shares in their portfolios.

Key Metrics to Understand

Earnings per share (EPS)

Earnings per share tells you how much profit a company made for each share on issue. If a company earned $10 million and has 10 million shares outstanding, its EPS is $1.00. It's a way of measuring profitability on a per-share basis so you can compare across companies of different sizes.

Price-to-earnings ratio (P/E)

The P/E ratio divides the current share price by the earnings per share. If a share trades at $20 and has earnings per share of $1, its P/E ratio is 20 — meaning investors are paying $20 for every $1 of current earnings.

A higher P/E generally means investors expect strong future growth. A lower P/E might indicate the company is undervalued, or it might signal the market has concerns about its future. Neither is automatically good or bad — context matters enormously. Comparing a company's P/E to its historical average, and to others in the same industry, gives you more useful information than the number in isolation.

Why Individual Shares Carry More Risk Than Index Funds

When you buy an index fund — such as one tracking the ASX 200 — you instantly own tiny portions of the 200 largest companies on the ASX. If one company collapses, it barely affects your overall holding. Your risk is spread broadly.

When you buy individual shares, you're making a concentrated bet. If you put 20% of your portfolio into one company and that company runs into trouble — accounting fraud, regulatory problems, a major competitor, or simply a bad strategic decision — you can lose a significant portion of your wealth. This is called concentration risk.

Research consistently shows that most active investors — including professionals — underperform simple index funds over the long run, particularly after fees and taxes are considered. Picking winning individual stocks is genuinely hard.

Individual shares also require ongoing attention. A company's circumstances change — management turns over, industries get disrupted, debt levels shift. Owning individual shares means you need to stay informed, revisit your thesis regularly, and be willing to sell if the reasons you bought no longer hold.

Common Mistakes Beginners Make

  • Buying based on recent performance. A share that's doubled in the past year isn't automatically a good buy — the opportunity may already be priced in, or the rise may be speculative.
  • Confusing a good company with a good investment. Well-known, admirable companies can still be overpriced. Paying too much for a great company can produce poor returns.
  • Ignoring diversification. Holding three or four shares in the same sector (say, four banks) doesn't provide real diversification.
  • Letting emotions drive decisions. Selling in a panic during a downturn, or buying into hype at a peak, are the most reliable ways to destroy wealth.
  • Underestimating transaction costs and tax. Each trade may involve brokerage fees, and selling shares for a profit triggers capital gains tax (CGT), which the ATO will expect you to report. Shares held for more than 12 months attract a 50% CGT discount for individuals.
  • Starting too big. Many beginners invest more
