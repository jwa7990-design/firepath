High interest savings accounts vs term deposits — which is better?

Part of the FirePath Learning Lab · Educational only, not financial advice · Browse all topics →

Two Safe Ways to Earn on Your Cash — But They Work Differently

When it comes to keeping your money safe while still earning a return, Australian savers generally have two main options sitting at the conservative end of the spectrum: high interest savings accounts and term deposits. Both are straightforward, both are government-protected up to a point, and both beat leaving money idle in an everyday transaction account. But they suit different situations, and understanding the difference can mean more money in your pocket without taking on any extra risk.

How High Interest Savings Accounts Work

A high interest savings account (sometimes called a HISA) is a deposit account offered by banks and online lenders that pays a higher rate of interest than a standard savings or transaction account. Your money stays accessible — you can deposit more or withdraw funds whenever you need — while still earning interest on your balance.

Most high interest savings accounts in Australia come with a catch: the advertised rate is often a bonus rate, meaning it only applies if you meet certain monthly conditions. Common requirements include depositing a minimum amount each month (say, $200 or $1,000), making no withdrawals, or linking the account to an eligible everyday account with the same bank. If you miss the conditions, you revert to a much lower base rate.

Interest is typically calculated daily and paid monthly. Because the Reserve Bank of Australia (RBA) sets the official cash rate, and banks broadly follow it, HISA rates tend to move up or down after each RBA decision.

Watch the bonus rate conditions closely. Some accounts advertise an attractive headline rate, but if you miss a single condition in a month, you could earn a fraction of that rate. Always check the base rate — that's your fallback.

How Term Deposits Work

A term deposit is a fixed-term agreement with a bank or authorised deposit-taking institution (ADI). You lock in a lump sum for a set period — anything from one month to five years — and the bank guarantees a fixed interest rate for the entire term. When the term ends (the maturity date), you receive your original deposit back plus the interest earned.

The trade-off is inflexibility. Unlike a savings account, you generally cannot access your money before the term ends without paying a break fee or reduced interest penalty. This makes term deposits better suited to money you genuinely won't need in a hurry.

Interest can be paid monthly, quarterly, annually, or at maturity depending on the product and term length. For longer terms, having interest paid periodically rather than at the end can be useful for cash flow — and worth factoring into your comparison.

Current Rate Context in Australia

Following the RBA's rate hiking cycle that began in May 2022, both savings account and term deposit rates rose significantly from the record lows of the pandemic era. At their peak, competitive HISAs were offering rates above 5% per annum, and term deposits for 12-month terms were sitting in a similar range for many of the major banks and online lenders.

As the RBA began cutting rates in 2025, these returns started softening. The key point is that rates are not permanent — a HISA rate can change at any time with little notice, while a term deposit locks in whatever rate you agreed to at the start. This dynamic is central to the choice between them.

For current rates, ASIC's MoneySmart website is a useful starting point for unbiased comparisons, as is checking directly with banks and online lenders such as ING, Macquarie, Ubank, and the major four banks (ANZ, CBA, NAB, Westpac).

Flexibility vs Rate: The Core Trade-Off

When a savings account makes more sense

  • You're building an emergency fund and need access at short notice
  • You're saving towards a goal with an uncertain timeline (e.g. a house deposit where settlement could vary)
  • You're receiving regular income you want to park temporarily
  • You expect interest rates to rise further and want to benefit from any increases
  • You prefer the ability to make additional deposits over time

When a term deposit makes more sense

  • You have a lump sum you won't need for a defined period
  • You want certainty — a guaranteed rate regardless of what the RBA does next
  • You expect rates to fall and want to lock in today's higher rate
  • You're a retiree or near-retiree who values predictable income
  • You want to remove the temptation to dip into savings

One practical strategy: Keep three to six months of living expenses in a flexible HISA for emergencies, and consider locking surplus cash into a term deposit once your buffer is set. This way you're not sacrificing access you might genuinely need.

The Government Deposit Guarantee

Both high interest savings accounts and term deposits held with Australian ADIs (banks, building societies, credit unions) are covered by the Financial Claims Scheme (FCS), administered by the Australian Prudential Regulation Authority (APRA). This government guarantee protects deposits up to $250,000 per person, per institution.

If you have more than $250,000 in cash savings, spreading deposits across multiple institutions means each balance is separately protected. This is worth considering for retirees, those who have recently sold a property, or anyone holding a large cash balance temporarily.

The Real Return: What Inflation Does to Your Savings

Here's the part that often gets overlooked. Even if a term deposit is paying 4.5% per annum, what matters is your real return — that is, your return after subtracting inflation. If inflation is running at 3.5%, your real return is only around 1% before tax.

The Australian Taxation Office (ATO) also requires you to declare interest income as part of your assessable income each financial year. At a marginal tax rate of 32.5% (plus the Medicare levy), a nominal 4.5% rate can become a real after-tax return that barely keeps pace with living costs. This doesn't mean cash saving is pointless — it absolutely has a role — but it does illustrate why relying on savings accounts or term deposits alone won't build serious long-term wealth.

The Bigger Picture: Cash Is a Tool, Not a Strategy

Cash savings serve a vital purpose: they protect capital, provide liquidity, and reduce anxiety. But history shows that over the long run, assets like Australian shares (ASX-listed equities), diversified managed funds, property, and — critically for most Australians — superannuation, tend to deliver meaningfully higher real returns than cash deposits.

Superannuation in particular offers tax-advantaged compound growth over decades. Making voluntary concessional contributions (up to the annual cap) into a well-chosen super fund often delivers better long-term outcomes than holding excess cash in even the best-paying savings account. That's not an argument against savings accounts or term deposits — it's an argument for using them where they genuinely fit: short-term goals, emergency reserves, and capital preservation.

Cash saving and investing are not either/or. A sensible financial position typically involves both: accessible cash for near-term needs and stability, alongside growth-oriented investments for the long term.

Putting It Together

High interest savings accounts and term deposits are both legitimate, low-risk tools for Australian savers. Savings accounts win on flexibility and ease; term deposits win on rate certainty and discipline. The right choice
